\abstract{We propose that gravity can be understood as the physical backreaction of quantum measurement. Standard results, horizon scales, entropy bounds, and the Bekenstein--Hawking area law, follow from Landauer's principle at the Unruh temperature as consequences of Newton's laws of motion and gravitation. To ground our idea, we extend a specific example from our previous work, a bilocal microstate construction where entangled photon pairs emitted from a localized source, propagate into opposite Rindler wedges. We show how polarisation measurements produce discrete energy-momentum impulses and correlated classical bits. Each such microstate is described geometrically at the horizon by an Aichelburg--Sexl pp-wave solution, whose longitudinal shift generates spatial separation between the wedges and whose transverse focusing increments the horizon area. The framework provides an operational realization of ER=EPR at the level of these individual microstates, and complements the thermodynamic approaches of Jacobson and Verlinde by supplying a microphysical process underlying each entropy increment.}

\section{Introduction}

Two landmark results establish that gravity and Unurh thermodynamics are deeply intertwined. Jacobson showed that the Einstein equations can be derived from thermodynamic identities applied to local Rindler horizons, with horizon entropy playing the role of the fundamental quantity from which spacetime dynamics emerge~\cite{jacobson1995thermodynamics}. Verlinde extended this perspective, arguing that gravitational forces arise from entropy gradients on holographic screens, recasting gravity as an entropic rather than a fundamental interaction~\cite{entropic}. Both frameworks, however, operate at the level of bulk thermodynamics and leave open a prior question: what physical process underlies each individual entropy increment?

We argue that the answer is quantum measurement. Each act of information acquisition by a physical observer constitutes a discrete microstate event, and the collective backreaction of such events, together with the causal correlations they induce, gives rise to gravitational effects. The argument proceeds in three steps. First, any observation is mediated by a physical interaction: information transfer necessarily involves an exchange of energy and momentum between the observed system and the detector, a discrete impulse. Second, by the equivalence principle, this impulse is locally indistinguishable in that from the effect of a gravitational field: no local measurement can distinguish the recoil associated with a quantum measurement event from acceleration in a gravitational potential. Third, when such impulses are modelled geometrically, via Aichelburg--Sexl (AS) pp-wave solutions, as we explore, they imprint energy and information directly onto horizon geometry, and coarse-graining over the resulting ensemble recovers standard horizon thermodynamics. A unifying structure runs through the relations, and it is worth pausing to discuss the general ideas involved before diving into our explicit example. 

\subsection{Conceptual Roadmap and Subjective Relations}
In each case in our argument, the relation between two adjacent concepts has the same logical character: every instance of the more abstract concept is grounded in an instance of the more physical one, but not every instance of the more physical concept gives rise to the more abstract one \emph{for a given observer}. The gap in each case is filled by subjectivity. Quantum state reduction is always backed by an observation, it is a Bayesian update on the observer's part, but an observation that carries no new information for that observer produces no state reduction. Equally, every observation is backed by an energy-momentum transfer, but not every such transfer constitutes an observation for you: an interaction involving a different ``observer'' leaves your knowledge unchanged. The Unruh effect suggests the same structure extends to the equivalence principle: an accelerating observer perceives a thermal bath and attributes it to a gravitational field, while an inertial observer sees only vacuum. Whether an acceleration counts as gravity is perspectival in precisely the same sense. In each case the relation is an identification, not a causal link, and the identification is observer-relative.

This stands in sharp contrast to the Di\'osi--Penrose objective collapse programme~\cite{penrose1996gravity, diosi1987universal}, which postulates that gravity \emph{causes} quantum state reduction as an objective, observer-independent physical process. Figure~\ref{fig:diagram} summarises this contrast. The three solid arrows represent the perspectival grounding relations described above; the single dashed arrow represents the Di\'osi--Penrose causal postulate. It is the only relation in the diagram claimed to be objective and observer-independent, and it is the only one we do not adopt.

To make this concrete, we build on the microstate construction of~\cite{player2025driving}, in which bilocal driving sources generate entangled Rindler excitations that realize individual terms of the Unruh thermal ensemble. The present paper applies and extends that construction. Entangled pairs emitted from a localised source propagate into left and right Rindler wedges; polarisation measurement events produce discrete energy-momentum impulses and correlated classical bits. We show that each such microstate carries a calculable gravitational imprint, modelled by an AS pp-wave solution, and that coarse-graining over the ensemble recovers standard horizon thermodynamics. This construction also provides an operational realization of ER=EPR at the level of individual microstates: entanglement is converted into geometric information, and each measurement event contributes a discrete, calculable increment to horizon entropy.

Jacobson's derivation of the Einstein equations from horizon thermodynamics and Verlinde's entropic force framework both operate at the level of bulk averages, leaving open the question of what physical process underlies each entropy increment. The present construction supplies that microstate realization: each entropy increment corresponds to a physically mediated measurement event with a calculable energy-momentum transfer. Thermality is emergent rather than intrinsic, arising from coarse-graining over an ensemble of source-driven microstates, each of which admits a definite, non-thermal description in the global Minkowski frame. The thermodynamic results of both programmes are recovered as a consequence of this coarse-graining, rather than assumed as a starting point.

A closely related perspective is provided by relational and information-theoretic interpretations of quantum theory. Carlo Rovelli’s relational quantum mechanics \cite{rovelli1996relational} and the QBist programme associated with Christopher Fuchs \cite{fuchs2014introduction} both treat the quantum state as observer-dependent, encoding information rather than objective physical reality. In these frameworks, measurement is understood as an update of an observer’s knowledge, not a universal physical collapse. The Unruh effect \cite{unruh1976notes} provides a direct physical analogue of this observer-relativity: particle content and thermality themselves become observer-dependent notions, tied to acceleration. Our construction extends this shared principle across domains, identifying quantum state reduction, thermality, and gravitational effects as different aspects of observer-relative descriptions grounded in underlying physical interactions. This viewpoint also sharpens the conceptual tension highlighted by Eugene Wigner \cite{wigner1995remarks}, where different observers may consistently assign different physical descriptions to the same process, suggesting that the resulting geometric imprints in our framework may likewise be observer-dependent. Finally, the present approach may be viewed as a concrete dynamical realization of Wheeler's 'it from bit' programme~\cite{wheeler2018information}. Where Wheeler's proposal remained largely conceptual, discrete acts of information acquisition giving rise to physical law, we identify the explicit dynamical mechanism: each bit acquired by a physical observer is backed by a measurement-induced energy-momentum transfer, modelled geometrically as an AS pp-wave, whose gravitational imprint incrementally builds spacetime structure. The bit does not merely describe the geometry; it produces it.

\section{Motivating Heuristics}
\label{sec:heuristics}

Landauer's principle associates a minimal energy cost with the acquisition of information \cite{landauer1961irreversibility}. For an accelerated observer, the relevant temperature is the Unruh temperature \cite{unruh1976notes}. Substituting one into the other eliminates temperature entirely, yielding a relation between information acquisition, energy transfer, and acceleration (a version of the BHT first law, equation (\ref{eq:UL})). This is not merely an algebraic convenience: it is a concrete dictionary between the thermal description natural to a Rindler observer and the non-thermal global Minkowski description, with the apparent thermality of the vacuum emerging as a consequence of the observer's acceleration rather than as a fundamental property of the state. In this way we derive a sequence of standard results in black hole thermodynamics as a consequence of Newton's laws.

\subsection{The Unruh-Landauer Relation}
Landauer's principle suggests that the acquisition of $\Delta H$ bits of information by a physical observer requires a minimum energy transfer
\begin{equation}
  \Delta E \geq k_B T \, \Delta H.
\end{equation}
We note that Landauer's principle is conventionally stated for information erasure rather than acquisition. Here we apply it to acquisition, which is the physically relevant process for an observer registering a measurement outcome. The bound has the same form in both cases; we apply it to acquisition on the grounds that registering a measurement outcome is physically equivalent to a bit being written into the detector's state, which is the process Landauer's principle governs.

For an observer undergoing proper acceleration $a$, the Unruh effect associates a temperature
\begin{equation}
T = \frac{\hbar a}{2 \pi k_B c}
\end{equation}
with the local vacuum. Substituting into Landauer's bound eliminates $k_B T$, yielding
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\Delta E \geq \frac{\hbar a}{2\pi c} \, \Delta H.}
\label{eq:UL}
\end{equation}
Temperature has dropped out entirely: equation~\eqref{eq:UL} is a temperature-independent relation between energy, acceleration, and information, suitable for the Minkowski vacuum. Acceleration sets the energy scale of information. In what follows, standard results emerge as consequences of this single relation and Newton's laws.
\subsection{Momentum Transfer and Acceleration}
The energy transfer $\Delta E$ implied by equation~\eqref{eq:UL} is carried by a physical quantum — matter or radiation — mediating the measurement interaction. We refer to this as the \emph{thrust quantum} of effective mass $m_t$. (This will actually be a pure energy carrier in our later microstate example.)
For a relativistic carrier, the associated momentum transfer is
\begin{equation}
\Delta p = \frac{\Delta E}{c}.
\end{equation}

We take the interaction time to be the order of the light-crossing time across the interaction region, $\Delta t \sim 2r/c$, corresponding to a causal exchange over the scale $r$. The numerical factor of 2 is not fixed a priori at this level; it will be determined by requiring consistency with the gravitational scale that emerges below. For a detector of mass $m_d$, Newton's second law in impulse form gives
\begin{equation}
a = \frac{\Delta p}{m_d \Delta t} = \frac{\Delta E}{2 m_d r}.
\label{eq:accel}
\end{equation}

This perspective is closely related in spirit to Erik Verlinde’s entropic gravity \cite{entropic}, where forces arise from entropy gradients, but differs in that the present construction grounds each entropy increment in a discrete measurement event with an associated energy-momentum transfer, rather than in a coarse-grained thermodynamic gradient.

\subsection{The Schwarzschild Radius}
We match the acceleration inferred from the measurement-induced impulse to the Newtonian gravitational field in order to identify the characteristic length scale associated with the detector. The thrust quantum carries energy $\Delta E$ and produces an acceleration $a$ on the detector. Identifying this with the Newtonian gravitational acceleration due to the mass $m_t$ at distance $r$.
\begin{equation}
a = \frac{G m_t}{r^2},
\end{equation}
and substituting equation ~\eqref{eq:accel} with $\Delta E=m_t c^2$, we obtain
\begin{equation}
\frac{m_t c^2}{2 m_d r} = \frac{G m_t}{r^2}.
\end{equation}
Solving for $r$:
\begin{equation}
r = \frac{2 G m_d}{c^2} \equiv r_s,
\end{equation}
which is precisely the Schwarzschild radius of the detector mass $m_d$. This fixes the factor of 2 in the interaction time retroactively: the choice $\Delta r = 2r/c$ is the unique one consistent with Newtonian gravity and the correct Schwarzschild scale.

It is notable that the resulting length scale is set by the detector mass $m_d$, rather than the source mass $m_t$. This reflects the fact that the construction is inherently observer-centred: the acceleration entering the argument is the response of the detector to the measurement-induced impulse, and the associated scale is therefore the horizon radius relevant to that observer. In this sense, the quantity $r_s$  should be interpreted not as the Schwarzschild radius of the source, but as the characteristic horizon scale associated with the detector as a physical system undergoing measurement. This is consistent with the observer-dependence of horizon structure in relativistic settings, as exemplified by the Unruh effect, where acceleration alone gives rise to a causal horizon.

\subsection{Surface Gravity}
Evaluating the acceleration at $r = r_s$ gives the surface gravity of the detector:
\begin{equation}
\kappa = \frac{G m_d}{r_s^2} = \frac{c^4}{4 G m_d},
\end{equation}
in agreement with the standard Schwarzschild result. This is the acceleration that appears in the Unruh temperature associated with the detector's horizon, $T = \hbar \kappa / 2\pi k_B c$, providing an internal consistency check on the framework.

\subsection{Bekenstein-Hawking Entropy}
Returning to the Unruh-Landauer relation~\eqref{eq:UL} and substituting the surface gravity $\kappa$ for $a$, the entropy increment associated with an energy increment $\Delta E = c^2 \Delta m_d$ is
\begin{equation}
\Delta H \leq \frac{2\pi c}{\hbar \kappa} \Delta E = \frac{8\pi G m_d}{\hbar c^3} \cdot c^2 \Delta m_d.
\end{equation}
Expressing this in terms of the change in Schwarzschild area $\Delta A = 32\pi G^2 m_d \Delta m_d / c^4$:
\begin{equation}
\Delta H \le \frac{\Delta A}{4 \ell_P^2}, \qquad \ell_P^2 = \frac{\hbar G}{c^3},
\end{equation}
which is the Bekenstein-Hawking area law. Each measurement event contributes a discrete entropy increment proportional to the area imprinted on the horizon by the associated energy-momentum transfer.

This derivation parallels Jacob Bekenstein’s original argument \cite{bekenstein}, in which the entropy increase of a black hole is inferred from the minimal energy required to localize and drop a system across the horizon; here, the same structure emerges from the minimal energy cost of registering information at the horizon scale.

The two bounds are saturated simultaneously when $a = c^2/R$ — which is exactly the condition for being {\it at a horizon}. The horizon is therefore not just where thermodynamics happens to work out, it is the unique locus where the minimum cost of information acquisition equals the maximum information content of the region.

\subsection{Summary}
Beginning from the single relation~\eqref{eq:UL}, and using only Newton's laws and the equivalence principle, we have recovered the Schwarzschild radius, the surface gravity, and the Bekenstein-Hawking area law as consequences of the observation $\rightarrow$ acceleration identification. These are standard results, but their derivation here from the physical cost of information acquisition rather than from thermodynamic postulates is the point.

The thrust quantum introduced in this section, the physical carrier of the measurement-induced impulse, will be given a explicit microstate realization in the sections that follow. It is identified with one of a pair of entangled photons modeled as Aichelburg-Sexl pp-waves crossing past Rindler horizons in the Unruh picture. In this case the back reaction of a single force carrier in one wedge is correlated with an equal and opposite reaction in the other from a common source.


\section{Bilocal Microstate Construction}
\label{sec:microstates}

Let $\hbar$ = $c$ = 1.

The heuristic arguments of Section~\ref{sec:heuristics} establish a relationship between information acquisition, acceleration, and gravitational effects, but leave open the question of what a single microstate looks like concretely. In standard treatments, the Unruh effect is derived by coupling a detector to a pre-existing acceleration and reading off a thermal response from the vacuum. Following~\cite{player2025driving}, we invert this logic: we place an interaction in the past, where a source injects non-thermal entangled excitations into the field, and show that the observer's accelerated response is driven by these source-induced excitations. The thermal ensemble then emerges as a coarse-grained aggregate of such microstates, rather than being a primitive property of the vacuum.

We now upgrade this construction from a spin-0 scalar field in the original source~\cite{player2025driving} to the spin-1 electromagnetic
field. This is not merely a technical generalisation. As we show, the photon carries a unit
of action $\hbar$ per cycle alongside its energy $E = \hbar\omega$, which closes the
Landauer gap left open in Section~\ref{sec:heuristics}: in each wedge, the minimum actionable event in the
construction is precisely a single photon, and its polarisation state constitutes the
classical bit acquired by each observer from detection. The anticorrelated helicities of the entangled pair
are the natural completion of the $\mathbb{Z}_2$ swap symmetry between wedges, and as we
discuss in Section~\ref{sec:ppwave}, they imprint a definite geometric signature onto the
horizon consistent with the ER=EPR identification.

\subsection{The Bilocal Source}
%------------------------------------------------------

In double-null gauge the electromagnetic field is characterised by its transverse components
alone. The bilocal driving source generalises the scalar construction to
\begin{equation}
    J^{ij}(x,y) = \lambda\; \epsilon^{i*}_{(-1)}\, \epsilon^{j}_{(+1)}\; f_L(x)\, f_R(y),
\label{eq:bilocal_vec}
\end{equation}
where $\lambda$ is a coupling constant, $f_L$ and $f_R$ are normalised mode profiles supported
on the left and right wedges respectively, and $\epsilon^i_{(\lambda)}$ are the circular
polarisation vectors
\begin{equation}
    \epsilon^i_{(+1)} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{pmatrix}1\\i\end{pmatrix}, \qquad
    \epsilon^i_{(-1)} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{pmatrix}1\\-i\end{pmatrix}.
\end{equation}
The index structure of~\eqref{eq:bilocal_vec} assigns helicity $+1$ to the right-moving mode
and helicity $-1$ to the left-moving mode: the pair is \emph{anticorrelated} in helicity. This
is the unique assignment consistent with angular momentum conservation at the emission vertex
and with the $\mathbb{Z}_2$ wedge-swap symmetry, under which $\epsilon^i_{(+1)} \leftrightarrow
\epsilon^i_{(-1)}$.

The sourced Lagrangian reads

\end{aligned}
\label{eq:bilocal_lag}
\end{equation}

The thrust quantum of Section~\ref{sec:heuristics} is thereby identified with a single photon: the minimum actionable event is the emission and detection of one such quantum.

carries exactly one quantum of action $\hbar$ and one binary helicity

$\ln 2$ nats, and these are paired by the construction independently 
of $a$. The Landauer bound then operates as a consistency condition on 
the ensemble: the minimum energy cost per bit acquired, summed over the 
rate of acquisition, equals the power required to sustain the acceleration.

\subsection{Polarisation Bogoliubov Transformation}
%------------------------------------------------------

We now show that the polarisation labels survive the Rindler--Unruh mode decomposition,
so that the thermal ensemble retains its helicity structure and the anticorrelation of the
emitted pair descends unambiguously to the level of individual Rindler microstates.

In the transverse plane, the electromagnetic field in double-null gauge decomposes into
two independent scalar-like sectors labelled by helicity $\lambda = \pm 1$. For each sector,
the right-wedge field expands as
\begin{equation}
    A^i_\lambda(x) = \int_0^\infty \frac{d\Omega}{2\pi}\,
        \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\Omega}}\,\epsilon^i_{(\lambda)}
        \left[
            b^R_{\Omega,\lambda}\, u^R_\Omega(x)
            + b^{R\dagger}_{\Omega,\lambda}\, u^{R*}_\Omega(x)
        \right],
\end{equation}
where $u^R_\Omega(x)$ are the right-wedge Rindler modes at Rindler frequency $\Omega$, and
$b^R_{\Omega,\lambda}$, $b^{R\dagger}_{\Omega,\lambda}$ are the corresponding annihilation
and creation operators satisfying
\begin{equation}
    \left[b^R_{\Omega,\lambda},\, b^{R\dagger}_{\Omega',\lambda'}\right]
    = \delta(\Omega - \Omega')\,\delta_{\lambda\lambda'}.
\end{equation}
An identical expansion holds in the left wedge with $R \to L$ and $\lambda \to -\lambda$,
reflecting the anticorrelated helicity assignment. Because each helicity sector is
independently a scalar-like sector, the Bogoliubov transformation relating Minkowski and
Rindler modes takes the same form in each sector. For helicity $\lambda$ at Rindler
frequency $\Omega$, the Unruh modes are
\begin{equation}
    c_{\Omega,\lambda} = \cosh\theta_\Omega\; b^R_{\Omega,\lambda}
                       - \sinh\theta_\Omega\; b^{L\dagger}_{\Omega,-\lambda},
\label{eq:bogoliubov}
\end{equation}
where $\tanh\theta_\Omega = e^{-\pi\Omega c/a}$ encodes the Unruh distribution at acceleration $a$, and $-\lambda$ on the left-wedge operator reflects the anticorrelation. The transformation~\eqref{eq:bogoliubov} is the standard Bogoliubov relation for a massless field~\cite{unruh1976notes, birrell1984quantum}, now carrying a helicity label throughout. The Minkowski vacuum in the Rindler basis reads, for each helicity sector,
\begin{equation}
    |0\rangle_M^{(\lambda)}
    = \frac{1}{\cosh\theta_\Omega}
      \sum_{n=0}^\infty \tanh^n\theta_\Omega\;
      |n_{\Omega,\lambda}\rangle_R\,
      |n_{\Omega,-\lambda}\rangle_L,
\label{eq:vacuum_vec}
\end{equation}
and the full vacuum is the product over all $\Omega$ and both helicities:
\begin{equation}
    |0\rangle_M = \prod_{\Omega,\lambda} |0\rangle_M^{(\lambda)}.
\end{equation}

Equation~\eqref{eq:vacuum_vec} makes explicit that the Minkowski vacuum is a sum over
entangled helicity-anticorrelated pairs at each Rindler frequency. The bilocal source
selects a single term from this sum at fixed $\Omega$ and fixed helicity pair
$(\lambda, -\lambda) = (+1,-1)$, yielding the single-microstate
\begin{equation}
    |\Psi_{\Omega}\rangle_M
    = |1_{\Omega,+1}\rangle_R\,|1_{\Omega,-1}\rangle_L
      + \mathcal{O}(\text{higher order}).
\label{eq:microstate_vec}
\end{equation}
Wright's detection of helicity $+1$ and Lester's detection of helicity $-1$ are therefore
perfectly anticorrelated: each measurement event yields one classical bit, and the two bits
are the complement of one another.

The key structural result is that the helicity label $\lambda$ is conserved under the
Bogoliubov transformation: it enters equation~\eqref{eq:bogoliubov} as a spectator index and
exits unchanged on the Unruh operators $c_{\Omega,\lambda}$. Thermality, when it arises from
coarse-graining over the ensemble of microstates~\eqref{eq:microstate_vec}, is therefore
helicity-resolved: each helicity sector thermalises independently, and the anticorrelation
between wedges is preserved at every level of the construction.

\section{Matter as Substrate}
\label{sec:substrate}

The Unruh–Landauer relation of Section~\ref{sec:heuristics} constrains the minimal energy required to register information, but does not specify where that information resides physically. The present section addresses this gap by identifying matter as the substrate in which such information is stored, with each act of acquisition corresponding to the deposition of a discrete quantum of action into the detector’s degrees of freedom. We show how information is encoded in matter and energy and is reflected in the horizon geometry. This requires a physical account of what it means for matter to \emph{carry} information.

A massive particle accumulates phase along its worldline at a rate set by its rest energy. The Compton frequency relation $f = E/h$ assigns a natural frequency to any mass $m$, and Bremermann's computational limit~\cite{bremermann1962optimization} independently recovers the same rate $f = mc^2/h$ as the maximum clock rate, or Nyquist rate, of any physical system with that energy $E$. These two results agree because they are both expressions of the same underlying fact: a quantum of action $\hbar$ is the minimal unit of physical evolution, and $mc^2/h$ is the rate at which a mass $m$ consumes such quanta.

Each such quantum corresponds to one sample\footnote{We use \emph{sample} throughout to denote a single zero crossing of the phase $e^{iS/\hbar}$; readers may equivalently read this as \emph{event} or \emph{tick}. It is only defined upto the Heisenberg resolution.} of the phase $e^{iS/\hbar}$ in the path integral, and classical trajectories emerge from the constructive interference of these samples across paths. Paths separated by less than one sample of action, less than $\hbar$, do 
not produce resolvable interference fringes: the phase difference is 
insufficient to complete a full cycle of constructive and destructive 
interference, so the trajectory cannot be distinguished at sub-sample 
resolution. The trajectory is resolved only down to the scale set by 
$\hbar/E$, which is precisely the Heisenberg uncertainty at energy $E$.
This corresponds to a natural ultraviolet cutoff given by the energy $E$ of the system itself. Higher energies only come into play when external systems interact with the energy $E$ system.

The distinction between massless and massive degrees of freedom is therefore operational: massless quanta transmit information, while massive systems retain it, accumulating the action quanta that constitute physical memory. A massless particle, the photon, has no rest frame, no proper time, and accumulates no action between emission and absorption. It does not sample. It therefore carries no persistent
memory and cannot serve as a substrate. Its role in the present construction is precisely
that of a pure information carrier. A single photon carries exactly two internal irreducible constituents, distinct in kind:

\begin{itemize}

\item \textbf{One quantum of action $\hbar$}: the physical transfer, 
deposited as energy $\hbar\omega$ into the detector, incrementing its 
Bremermann sample rate by $\Delta f = \hbar\omega/h$ for as long as the 
energy remains in the mass. This increment is the physical trace of the 
interaction in the detector's matter-memory.
\item \textbf{One bit of polarisation}: the informational outcome, the 
classical record of which helicity, $\lambda = +1$ or $\lambda = -1$ 
--- was absorbed. This bit persists in the detector's state for as long 
as the detector retains the record. Erasure of this bit carries a minimum 
energy cost governed by Landauer's principle, regardless of how the 
record was originally established.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Types of sample}
  
Not every sample produces information, and not all information is produced the same way. We distinguish 
three classes of sample, ordered by their informational character.
\begin{itemize}
\item {\bf Absorption samples} are measurement events: a photon is 
detected, one quantum of action $\hbar$ is deposited persistently into the 
detector's matter-memory, and one helicity outcome is written as a classical 
bit. The Landauer cost $\Delta E \geq k_B T \ln 2$ is paid immediately and 
in full on acquisition. This is the sample category that drives the bilocal 
construction: Wright and Lester each undergo exactly one absorption sample per 
emitted pair, and it is these samples whose gravitational imprint is modelled 
by the AS pp-wave in Section~\ref{sec:ppwave}.

\item {\bf Free samples} are pure phase accumulation events: a massive particle 
propagates along its worldline, incrementing its sample count at the Bremermann 
rate $mc^2/h$, without writing any bit into any detector's state. No Landauer 
cost is incurred, no classical record is produced, and no observer acquires 
information. These are the samples that constitute mass and proper time in the 
background sense, the substrate on which absorption and emission events act.

\item {\bf Emission samples} are the time-reverse of absorption: a bit stored in 
matter-memory is released, the corresponding quantum of action is re-emitted 
as radiation, and the Landauer cost is paid on the way \emph{out} rather than 
on the way in. This is the category Landauer's principle most directly 
governs in its original erasure formulation.
\end{itemize}

A moving mirror realizes absorption and emission as reflection: it
receives a photon, stores the helicity bit transiently in its 
matter degrees of freedom during a period of free sample accumulation, and 
re-emits the photon, paying the erasure cost at emission. An ensemble of 
such mirrors, say two silvered mirrors  back to back, can cycle through absorption, free accumulation, and 
emission without ever producing a permanent classical record.

It is worth noting that the absorption/free/emission classification 
is observer-relative in the same sense as quantum state reduction 
itself. What constitutes an irreversible absorption sample for one 
observer may be a free sample for an external observer. If Wright truly forgets the helicity 
outcome, in the sense that his matter-memory becomes disentangled 
from the bit, the photon can interfere with its partner at a 
future Paz. The classification is not a property of the interaction 
itself but of the observer's epistemic access to the outcome. True 
irreversibility is approached asymptotically as the bit becomes 
correlated with an increasing number of environmental degrees of 
freedom, which is the standard decoherence picture now applied to 
the sample classification.

The $\hbar$ is what makes the sample physical; the bit is what 
makes it meaningful to an observer. These are distinct: the action 
quantum is deposited regardless of whether a persistent record is 
produced, while the bit exists only relative to an observer for 
whom the outcome is accessible. That a single photon carries one 
of each in the minimal case is the reason it is the natural unit 
of the construction, but neither the action nor the bit should be 
understood as intrinsic properties of the photon in isolation.

\subsection{Penrose's Zig-Zag Mechanism: Why Matter Samples}
\label{sec:zigzag}

The matter substrate argument of the preceding subsection established that massive particles
sample at the Bremermann rate $mc^2/h$ and that each sample is a quantum of action $\hbar$
accumulated in the path integral phase $e^{iS/\hbar}$. But it left open a prior question:
\emph{why} does matter sample at all? The zig-zag\footnote{Penrose calls these particle {\it zig-zag} particles, we are tempted to call them {\it tick-tock} particles, but only in a footnote.} construction of Penrose and Dirac supplies
a concrete physical answer, and in doing so reveals that the bilocal microstate construction
of Section~\ref{sec:microstates} is not merely analogous to the origin of mass, it is
structurally identical to it at the level of individual events.

\subsubsection{The Dirac 4-Spinor as a Bilocal System}

The Lorentz algebra decomposes as
\begin{equation}
    \mathfrak{so}(3,1) \cong \mathfrak{su}(2) \times \mathfrak{su}(2),
\end{equation}
where the two $\mathfrak{su}(2)$ factors correspond to the two helicities: the first is
generated by boosts combined with counter-clockwise rotations, and the second by boosts
combined with clockwise rotations~\cite{ryder1996quantum}. A Dirac 4-spinor decomposes under this
algebra into two Weyl 2-spinors of opposite helicity $\lambda = \pm\frac{1}{2}$ and
equal and opposite momenta. Penrose~\cite{penrose2006road} proposes a physical interpretation
of this decomposition: the two 2-spinors are real physical objects, each moving
instantaneously at the speed of light in opposite directions, interacting at regular
intervals via the Higgs field at the De Broglie frequency.

The structural parallel with the bilocal construction is very close:

\begin{itemize}
    \item The two Weyl 2-spinors, moving at $c$ in opposite directions with opposite
    helicity, correspond to the two photons emitted by Paz into the left and right
    Rindler wedges.
    \item The Higgs field plays the role of Wright and Lester: it is the common mediator whose
    interaction reflects the entangled pair and sets the interaction rate.
    \item Between Higgs interactions, each 2-spinor is massless and moves at $c$,
    carrying no persistent memory, pure information carriers, exactly as the photons
    in the bilocal construction.
    \item The Higgs interaction event itself is the measurement event: it deposits one
    quantum of action $\hbar$, flips the active helicity from $+\frac{1}{2}$ to
    $-\frac{1}{2}$ and back, and produces one sample of proper time.
\end{itemize}

Mass emerges from the regularity of these interactions: a particle is massive because it
undergoes repeated Higgs interactions at the De Broglie frequency, and its mass is
proportional to that frequency via $E = \hbar\omega$. The Bremermann sample rate $mc^2/h$
is therefore not a coincidence or a heuristic, it is the Higgs interaction rate,
the rate at which the zig-zag mechanism deposits quanta of action into the particle's
proper time.

\subsubsection{Mass as Accumulated Measurement History}

Between Higgs interactions, the two 2-spinors are massless and moving at $c$ in opposite
null directions. Each spinor therefore has a local Rindler structure relative to the other:
from the perspective of the right-moving spinor, the left-moving spinor is behind a causal
horizon, and vice versa. The Higgs interaction is the event that collapses this local
horizon and produces one sample, one unit of proper time, exactly as the photon
absorption by Wright collapses the Rindler horizon and produces one sample in the bilocal
construction.

This gives a precise sense in which mass is an accumulated measurement history. Each Higgs
interaction is a measurement event: it deposits
one quantum of action $\hbar$ into the particle's matter-memory, persistently incrementing
its sample rate by $\Delta f = \Delta E/h$. The particle's mass is the total accumulated
record of all such interactions since its formation. Matter is not just the substrate for
information, it \emph{is} information, in the sense that its mass encodes the accumulated
history of its Higgs interaction events.

\subsection{Matter as a Physical Channel}
\label{sec:channel}

The sampling rate and dynamic range of matter can be combined into 
a channel capacity in the Shannon sense. For a system of mass $m$ 
with internal Hilbert space dimension $d$, the maximum rate of 
information flow is

\begin{equation}
    C = \underbrace{\frac{mc^2}{h}}_{\text{bandwidth}} 
        \times 
        \underbrace{\log_2(d)}_{\text{dynamic range}}
        \quad \text{(bits per second),}
    \label{eq:channel_capacity}
\end{equation}
where the bandwidth is the Bremermann sampling rate and the dynamic 
range is the information content per sample. The product 
$C$ is the \emph{channel capacity} of the matter substrate: the 
maximum rate at which physical interactions can write information 
into its degrees of freedom.

The three sample types of Section~\ref{sec:substrate} correspond 
to three distinct regimes of the data processing inequality 
$H(\text{out}) \leq H(\text{in})$, which states that information 
cannot be created by physical processes, only preserved or lost:

\begin{equation}
\text{Free:} \quad H(\text{out}) = H(\text{in}), 
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\text{Absorption:} \quad H(\text{field, out}) < H(\text{field, in}),
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\text{Emission:} \quad H(\text{detector, out}) < H(\text{detector, in}).
\end{equation}

Free samples saturate the inequality: no information is lost or 
gained, the channel runs at capacity without transmitting anything. 
Absorption samples strictly decrease the field's information content 
as it flows into the detector: the channel is being \emph{used}, 
information is being written into matter-memory at a rate bounded 
by $C$. Emission samples strictly decrease the detector's information 
content as the stored bit is released back into the field: the 
channel runs in reverse, and the Landauer cost is the energy 
price of that reversal.

The data processing inequality is therefore the information-theoretic 
statement of the thermodynamic arrow of time in this framework: 
absorption and emission are the two directions of irreversible 
information flow, and free samples are the reversible background 
against which both are measured. The full quantum channel structure 
of the Bogoliubov transformation, its classical capacity in the 
Holevo--Schumacher--Westmoreland, we leave for future work.

The Bekenstein bound $H_\text{Bekenstein} \sim Gm^2/\hbar c$ 
has the same units and parametric dependence as $C \cdot \tau$ 
for proper time $\tau \sim Gm/c^3$ at the horizon scale, 
consistent with the channel capacity integrated over the 
light-crossing time. We make no stronger claim than this 
order-of-magnitude agreement; the precise numerical relationship 
depends on the dynamic range $\log_2(d)$ and is left for 
future work.

\section{pp-Wave Geometry}
\label{sec:ppwave}

\subsection{The AS pp-Wave and Its Effects}

Let $u = t - z$ and $v = t + z$, where the photon propagates along the null plane $u = 0$, $z$ is the longitudinal direction, and $(x,y)$ are transverse coordinates. Test particles at fixed transverse positions $(x,y)$ experience two effects as the shock front crosses $u = 0$:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Longitudinal shift.} The $v$ coordinate is displaced by
    \begin{equation}
    \Delta v \propto -\ln r^2, \qquad r^2 = x^2 + y^2.
    \end{equation}
    This shift is lightlike: no proper time elapses for a particle crossing the shock, but it is displaced along the null direction. Near the axis ($r \to 0$) the shift diverges, encoding the concentration of energy along the photon's trajectory.

    \item \textbf{Transverse focusing.} The gradient of the longitudinal shift produces a transverse velocity
    \begin{equation}
    \Delta \mathbf{v}_\perp \propto \nabla \ln r \propto \frac{1}{r},
    \end{equation}
    directed toward the axis. Test particles are pulled toward the centre of the wave, regardless of their initial transverse separation.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{The Longitudinal Shift Makes Space}

The longitudinal shift deserves closer attention, because it carries a meaning beyond a mere coordinate displacement. The photon emitted by Paz propagates along the null direction; as it crosses the Rindler horizon, the AS shift displaces the future light cone of every test particle it passes. For the photon traveling into the right wedge, the shift pushes the right wedge's geometry in the $+v$ direction; for the entangled partner traveling into the left wedge, the shift pushes the left wedge in the $-v$ direction. The two wedges are displaced \emph{away from each other} along the longitudinal null direction.

This is not merely a kinematic effect. \section{Appendix: Consistency of the Double-Null Gauge Condition}
% Verification that double-null gauge does not over-constrain physical degrees of freedom

We verify that the double-null gauge condition $A_+ = A_- = 0$ is consistent with 
Maxwell's equations and leaves exactly two physical degrees of freedom.

\subsection{Coordinates and Notation}

In double-null coordinates $(x^+, x^-, x^1, x^2)$ the Minkowski metric reads
\begin{equation}
    ds^2 = -2\,dx^+\,dx^- + (dx^1)^2 + (dx^2)^2,
\end{equation}
so that $\partial_+ = \partial/\partial x^+$, $\partial_- = \partial/\partial x^-$, and 
the d'Alembertian is
\begin{equation}
    \Box = -2\partial_+\partial_- + \partial_i\partial^i, \qquad i = 1,2.
\end{equation}
The gauge field has components $A_\mu = (A_+, A_-, A_1, A_2)$ and transforms as
\begin{equation}
    A_\mu \to A_\mu + \partial_\mu\Lambda
\end{equation}
under a gauge transformation with parameter $\Lambda(x^+, x^-, x^1, x^2)$.

\subsection{Imposing the Gauge Conditions in Stages}

The single gauge parameter $\Lambda$ is a function of all four coordinates. We use 
it in three stages, each consuming a different functional dependence.

\paragraph{Stage 1: $A_+ = 0$.}
Requiring $A_+ + \partial_+\Lambda = 0$ determines $\Lambda$ as a function of $x^+$:
\begin{equation}
    \Lambda(x^+, x^-, x^i) = -\int^{x^+} A_+(s, x^-, x^i)\,ds 
                              + \Lambda_0(x^-, x^i),
\end{equation}
where $\Lambda_0(x^-, x^i)$ is an arbitrary residual function independent of $x^+$.

\paragraph{Stage 2: $A_- = 0$.}
Under the residual freedom $\Lambda_0(x^-, x^i)$, the $A_-$ component transforms as
$A_- \to A_- + \partial_-\Lambda_0$. Setting this to zero determines $\Lambda_0$ as a 
function of $x^-$:
\begin{equation}
    \Lambda_0(x^-, x^i) = -\int^{x^-} A_-(s, x^i)\,ds + \Lambda_1(x^i),
\end{equation}
where $\Lambda_1(x^i)$ is a residual function of the transverse coordinates only.

\paragraph{Stage 3: Transverse Coulomb condition $\partial^i A_i = 0$.}
Under the remaining freedom $\Lambda_1(x^i)$, the transverse components transform as 
$A_i \to A_i + \partial_i\Lambda_1$. Imposing $\partial^i A_i = 0$ requires
\begin{equation}
    \nabla^2_\perp\Lambda_1 = -\partial^i A_i,
\end{equation}
which is a Poisson equation in the transverse plane with a unique solution (up to 
harmonic functions, fixed by boundary conditions). This exhausts the gauge freedom 
entirely.

The three conditions have consumed the full gauge parameter $\Lambda$ in stages, 
with no over-determination: each stage uses a strictly different functional 
dependence of $\Lambda$.

\subsection{Physical Degrees of Freedom}

After imposing $A_+ = A_- = 0$ and $\partial^i A_i = 0$, the remaining components 
are $A_1$ and $A_2$ subject to one differential constraint $\partial^i A_i = 0$. 
In momentum space this reads $k^i A_i = 0$.

For our specific construction, the photons propagate in the $\pm z$ direction, 
so their wave-vectors are purely null: $k^i_\perp = 0$ for $i = 1,2$. The 
transverse Coulomb condition $k^i A_i = 0$ is therefore trivially satisfied for 
any $A_1, A_2$, and both transverse components are independent. The two circular 
polarisation vectors
\begin{equation}
    \epsilon^i_{(+1)} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{pmatrix}1\\i\end{pmatrix}, \qquad
    \epsilon^i_{(-1)} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{pmatrix}1\\-i\end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
are the two independent physical degrees of freedom, corresponding to helicity 
$\lambda = \pm 1$. This is the correct count for a massless spin-1 field in four 
dimensions.

\subsection{Consistency with Maxwell's Equations}

With $A_+ = A_- = 0$ and $\partial^i A_i = 0$, the non-zero field strength 
components are
\begin{equation}
    F_{+i} = \partial_+ A_i, \qquad F_{-i} = \partial_- A_i, \qquad 
    F_{ij} = \partial_i A_j - \partial_j A_i, \qquad F_{+-} = 0.
\end{equation}
Maxwell's equations $\partial^\mu F_{\mu\nu} = J_\nu$ decompose as follows.

\paragraph{Transverse components $\nu = i$:}
\begin{equation}
    -2\partial_+\partial_- A_i + \partial^j(\partial_j A_i - \partial_i A_j) = J_i.
\end{equation}
Using $\partial^i A_i = 0$, the second term simplifies to $\partial^j\partial_j A_i$, 
giving the standard wave equation
\begin{equation}
    \boxed{\Box A_i = J_i.}
    \label{eq:wave}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Null components $\nu = \pm$:}
\begin{equation}
    -\partial^i\partial_\pm A_i = J_\pm.
\end{equation}
These are constraint equations relating the null source components to the transverse 
field. For our bilocal source, which is purely transverse, $J_+ = J_- = 0$, and the 
constraints reduce to
\begin{equation}
    \partial^i\partial_\pm A_i = 0.
\end{equation}
Differentiating the transverse Coulomb condition $\partial^i A_i = 0$ with respect 
to $x^\pm$ gives $\partial^i(\partial_\pm A_i) = 0$, so these constraints are 
automatically satisfied. \checkmark

\subsection{Summary}

The double-null gauge condition $A_+ = A_- = 0$ supplemented by the transverse 
Coulomb condition $\partial^i A_i = 0$:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item uses the gauge freedom of $\Lambda$ completely and without over-determination,
          via a three-stage procedure consuming the $x^+$-, $x^-$-, and $x^i$-dependent
          parts of $\Lambda$ in sequence;
    \item leaves exactly two physical degrees of freedom, the helicity eigenstates
          $\lambda = \pm 1$, consistent with the known count for a massless spin-1 field;
    \item reduces Maxwell's equations to the standard transverse wave equation
          $\Box A_i = J_i$, with the null constraint equations automatically satisfied
          for a purely transverse source.
\end{enumerate}
The gauge is therefore internally consistent and does not over-constrain the physical 
degrees of freedom. The $\mathbb{Z}_2$ wedge-swap symmetry is preserved throughout: 
conditions on $A_+$ and $A_-$ are imposed symmetrically, and neither null direction 
is privileged over the other.

\section{Appendix: Emergent Spacetime from Samples and Correlations}
\label{sec:emergent_spacetime}

The bilocal construction of Section~\ref{sec:microstates} and the matter substrate argument
of Section~\ref{sec:substrate} together suggest a picture in which spacetime is not a
background arena but an emergent property of matter and its correlations. We develop this
picture here, drawing on~\cite{player2025spacetime}. The central claim is that time and
space are distinct emergent phenomena with distinct physical origins: time emerges from the
sample count of a single massive particle, and space emerges from the phase difference between
sample counts of two or more correlated systems. Together they give emergent spacetime without
assuming a geometric background.

%------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Emergent Time: Samples as Proper Time}
%------------------------------------------------------

A massive particle at rest oscillates at the Compton frequency
\begin{equation}
    \omega = \frac{mc^2}{\hbar},
\end{equation}
and accumulates phase $e^{i\omega t}$ along its worldline. We define the \emph{sample set}
of the particle between proper times $\tau_1$ and $\tau_2$ as the set of zero crossings of
this phase:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{T}_{\tau_1,\tau_2} = \left\{\tau \in [\tau_1,\tau_2] : \tau \in
    \frac{2\pi}{\omega}\mathbb{Z}\right\}.
\end{equation}
The cardinality $|\mathcal{T}|$ is the sample count between the two events. This is a
Lorentz-invariant quantity: it is the number of times the particle's phase completes a full
cycle, which is an intrinsic property of the worldline independent of any external observer.
Proper time is the continuum limit of sample counting, and the Compton frequency $\omega =
mc^2/\hbar$ is simultaneously the Bremermann rate, the maximum sample rate for a system
of energy $mc^2$, and the Nyquist rate for the matter channel.

The sample count admits a natural Hilbert space description. We define
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{H}(T) = \mathrm{Span}\left\{|0\rangle, |1\rangle, |2\rangle, \ldots\right\},
\end{equation}
where $|n\rangle$ represents an observable sample count of $n$. The position operator
$\hat{N}|n\rangle = n|n\rangle$ is the sample number operator, and its conjugate under the
discrete Fourier transform is the frequency operator on the circle group $S^1$, with the
Nyquist frequency as the maximum. Wavefunctions $\psi(n)$ on $\mathcal{H}(T)$ describe
superpositions of sample counts, and classical proper time emerges from the expectation value
$\langle\hat{N}\rangle$ in states sharply peaked around large $n$.

\subsection{Emergent Space: Phase Difference as Spatial Separation}
%------------------------------------------------------

A single particle's sample count gives time but not space. Space requires at least two
correlated systems. Consider a particle in quantum superposition traversing two spatially
separated paths, with sample counts $n_1$ and $n_2$ accumulated along each path. The
interference pattern produced when the paths recombine is labelled by the fringe index
\begin{equation}
    k = n_1 - n_2,
\label{eq:fringe}
\end{equation}
which is the sample difference between the two paths. Each value of $k$ corresponds to a
distinct fringe of constructive interference, and the spatial separation between fringes
is set by the Compton wavelength $\lambda = h/mc$. The fringe index $k$ is therefore a
spatial quantum number: it labels a position in space relative to the interference pattern,
and space emerges as the eigenvalue spectrum of the operator
\begin{equation}
    \hat{K} = \hat{N}_1 - \hat{N}_2
\end{equation}
acting on the joint Hilbert space
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{H}(T_1, T_2) = \mathcal{H}(T_1) \otimes \mathcal{H}(T_2)
    = \bigoplus_{k \in \mathbb{Z}} \mathcal{P}_k,
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{P}_k = \mathrm{Span}\{|i\rangle \otimes |j\rangle : i - j = k\}$ is the
eigenspace of $\hat{K}$ with eigenvalue $k$~\cite{player2025spacetime}. The spatial
position is the eigenvalue of $\hat{K}$; it is not assumed but derived from the relative
sample count.

The structure of $\mathcal{H}(T_1, T_2)$ makes an important point: we do not symmetrise
the tensor product, because $|i\rangle \otimes |j\rangle$ and $|j\rangle \otimes |i\rangle$
represent physically distinct configurations, the particle went through path 1 first in
one case and path 2 first in the other. The two sample counts are distinguishable, and space
inherits that distinguishability.

Curvature enters naturally in this language. In flat spacetime, the sample counts $n_1,
\ldots, n_m$ for $m$ paths are linearly related: any $n_i$ is a linear combination of
$n_1$ and $n_2$~\cite{player2025spacetime}. In curved spacetime, the geodesic paths between
points acquire different lengths, and the sample counts are nonlinearly related in precisely
the way that encodes the curvature. Gravity, in this picture, is the statement that the
sample relationships between correlated matter systems are nonlinear, and the Einstein
equations are the consistency conditions on those nonlinear relationships.

\section{Unifying the Saturation Conditions}
\label{sec:unifying}

The heuristics of Section~\ref{sec:heuristics} and the sample picture of 
Section~\ref{sec:emergent_spacetime} each arrive independently at a 
saturation condition that singles out the horizon. We now show these are 
the same condition expressed in three complementary languages: 
thermodynamic, energetic, and information-theoretic.

\subsection{The Energy Ratio $\alpha$}

For a physical system of mass $m$ with characteristic radius $R$, define
\begin{equation}
    \alpha \equiv \frac{E_\hbar}{E_G} 
    = \frac{mc^2}{Gm^2/R} 
    = \frac{c^2 R}{Gm},
    \label{eq:alpha_def}
\end{equation}
where $E_\hbar = mc^2$ is the rest energy, which sets the Bremermann 
sample rate $f = mc^2/h$, and $E_G = Gm^2/R$ is the gravitational 
self-energy of the configuration. The ratio $\alpha$ is dimensionless 
and measures how far the system is from the regime in which quantum and 
gravitational energy scales coincide.

Physical configurations accessible to an external observer satisfy 
$E_\hbar \geq E_G$, equivalently $\alpha \geq 1$: a system with 
$E_G > E_\hbar$ would lie within its own Schwarzschild radius and be 
causally inaccessible. Setting $\alpha = 1$ gives
\begin{equation}
    R_{\alpha=1} = \frac{Gm}{c^2} \sim r_s = \frac{2Gm}{c^2},
    \label{eq:alpha_horizon}
\end{equation}
which is the Schwarzschild radius up to an $O(1)$ numerical factor 
reflecting the conventional choice of $E_G$. As with the factor of 2 
absorbed into the interaction time $\Delta t = 2r/c$ in 
Section~\ref{sec:heuristics}, this factor is fixed by requiring 
consistency with Newtonian gravity at the horizon scale; it does not 
affect the parametric identification.

\subsection{Three Languages for the Same Condition}

The condition $\alpha = 1$ is the same physical statement as the 
Landauer saturation condition of Section~\ref{sec:heuristics} and the 
sample-accumulation limit of Section~\ref{sec:emergent_spacetime}. 
The three descriptions are:

\paragraph{Thermodynamic language.}
The Unruh--Landauer bound and the Bekenstein--Hawking area law saturate 
simultaneously when $a = c^2/R$. Using the conversion between Boltzmann 
entropy and Shannon bits,
\begin{equation}
    S_B = k_B \ln 2 \cdot H,
    \label{eq:SB_H}
\end{equation}
the Unruh--Landauer bound~\eqref{eq:UL} expressed in bits reads
\begin{equation}
    \Delta E \geq \frac{\hbar a \ln 2}{2\pi c}\,\Delta H,
    \label{eq:UL_bits}
\end{equation}
and saturates when $a = c^2/R \sim c^4/Gm$, i.e.\ at 
$R \sim Gm/c^2 \sim r_s$. The horizon is the unique locus where the 
minimum energy cost per bit acquired equals the maximum information 
content per unit area.

\paragraph{Energetic language.}
The ratio $\alpha = E_\hbar/E_G$ reaches its minimum value of unity at 
$R \sim r_s$. At this point the quantum energy available for sample 
accumulation exactly equals the gravitational self-energy: no further 
information can be written into the system by an external observer 
without the system collapsing. The inequality $\alpha \geq 1$ is the 
energetic form of the holographic bound, the information capacity of 
the system, set by $E_\hbar$, must be at least as large as the 
gravitational cost of sustaining the configuration, set by $E_G$.

\paragraph{Sample language.}
The Bremermann rate $f = mc^2/h$ sets the maximum rate at which matter 
accumulates quanta of action $\hbar$ as samples. The gravitational 
self-energy $E_G \sim Gm^2/R$ sets the scale at which samples in one 
region of matter are gravitationally correlated with samples in another. 
At $\alpha = 1$ these two rates coincide: the intrinsic sample rate of the 
matter equals the rate at which the gravitational field can propagate 
correlations between samples. The horizon is therefore the surface at which 
the local information processing rate and the global gravitational 
coupling are in balance.

\subsection{The Horizon as a Fixed Point}

These three descriptions converge on a single structural statement: 
\emph{the horizon is the fixed point of the map between quantum 
information content and gravitational geometry}. Away from the horizon, 
$\alpha > 1$: the matter has more information capacity than the 
gravitational field accounts for, and space is created sample by sample as 
described in Section~\ref{sec:ppwave}. At the horizon, $\alpha = 1$: 
the two accounts balance exactly, the Landauer bound~\eqref{eq:UL_bits} 
saturates, the Bekenstein--Hawking area law is tight, and the 
sample-to-gravitational-energy ratio reaches its minimum. 

The $\alpha$ ratio makes the thermodynamic statement of 
Section~\ref{sec:heuristics} precise in energetic terms, and the sample 
language makes it precise in terms of the physical process, matter 
accumulating correlated quanta of action, that underlies both. All 
three are expressions of the same fixed-point condition: the horizon is 
not where thermodynamics happens to work out, but the unique locus where 
the minimum cost of information acquisition, the maximum information 
content of the region, and the balance between quantum and gravitational 
energy scales are simultaneously achieved.
