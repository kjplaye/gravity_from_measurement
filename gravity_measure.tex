\section{Introduction}

Two landmark results establish that gravity and Unurh thermodynamics are deeply intertwined. Jacobson showed that the Einstein equations can be derived from thermodynamic identities applied to local Rindler horizons, with horizon entropy playing the role of the fundamental quantity from which spacetime dynamics emerge~\cite{jacobson1995thermodynamics}. Verlinde extended this perspective, arguing that gravitational forces arise from entropy gradients on holographic screens, recasting gravity as an entropic rather than a fundamental interaction~\cite{entropic}. Both frameworks, however, operate at the level of bulk thermodynamics and leave open a prior question: what physical process underlies each individual entropy increment?

We argue that the answer is quantum measurement. Each act of information acquisition by a physical observer constitutes a discrete microstate event, and the collective backreaction of such events, together with the causal correlations they induce, gives rise to gravitational effects. The argument proceeds in three steps. First, any observation is mediated by a physical interaction: information transfer necessarily involves an exchange of energy and momentum between the observed system and the detector, a discrete impulse. Second, by the equivalence principle, this impulse is locally indistinguishable in that from the effect of a gravitational field: no local measurement can distinguish the recoil associated with a quantum measurement event from acceleration in a gravitational potential. Third, when such impulses are modelled geometrically, via Aichelburg--Sexl (AS) pp-wave solutions, as we explore, they imprint energy and information directly onto horizon geometry, and coarse-graining over the resulting ensemble recovers standard horizon thermodynamics.

\subsection{Conceptual Roadmap}
Our conceptual argument is a relation between adjacent concepts that have the same logical character: every instance of the more abstract concept is grounded in an instance of the more physical one, but not every instance of the more physical concept gives rise to the more abstract one \emph{for a given observer}. The gap in each case is filled by subjectivity. Quantum state reduction is always backed by an observation, it is a Bayesian update on the observer's part, but an observation that carries no new information for that observer produces no state reduction. Equally, every observation is backed by an energy-momentum transfer, but not every such transfer constitutes an observation for you: an interaction involving a different ``observer'' leaves your knowledge unchanged. The Unruh effect suggests the same structure extends to the equivalence principle: an accelerating observer perceives a thermal bath and attributes it to a gravitational field, while an inertial observer sees only vacuum. Whether an acceleration counts as gravity is perspectival in an 
analogous but distinct sense: not epistemic, as with Bayesian 
update, nor operational, as with energy--momentum transfer, but 
\emph{geometric}, underdetermined by any local measurement 
rather than by the observer's knowledge or participation. For a 
Rindler observer, the perceived gravitational field just is the 
Unruh thermal bath, which has no observer-independent meaning. In 
each case the relation is an identification rather than a causal 
link, and the identification is local and observer-relative, though 
the sense of observer-relativity differs across the three arrows.

This stands in sharp contrast to the Di\'osi--Penrose objective collapse programme~\cite{penrose1996gravity, diosi1987universal}, which postulates that gravity \emph{causes} quantum state reduction as an objective, observer-independent physical process. Figure~\ref{fig:diagram} summarises this contrast. The three solid arrows represent the perspectival grounding relations described above; the single dashed arrow represents the Di\'osi--Penrose causal postulate. It is the only relation in the diagram claimed to be objective and observer-independent, and it is the only one we do not adopt.

\subsection{Subjectivity and Existing Ontologies}

A closely related perspective is provided by relational and information-theoretic interpretations of quantum theory. Carlo Rovelli’s relational quantum mechanics \cite{rovelli1996relational} and the QBist programme associated with Christopher Fuchs \cite{fuchs2014introduction} both treat the quantum state as observer-dependent, encoding information rather than objective physical reality. In these frameworks, measurement is understood as an update of an observer’s knowledge, not a universal physical collapse. The Unruh effect \cite{unruh1976notes} provides a direct physical analogue of this observer-relativity: particle content and thermality themselves become observer-dependent notions, tied to acceleration. Our construction extends this shared principle across domains, identifying quantum state reduction, thermality, and gravitational effects as different aspects of observer-relative descriptions grounded in underlying physical interactions. This viewpoint also sharpens the conceptual tension highlighted by Eugene Wigner \cite{wigner1995remarks}, where different observers may consistently assign different physical descriptions to the same process, suggesting that the resulting geometric imprints in our framework may likewise be observer-dependent. Finally, the present approach may be viewed as a concrete dynamical realization of Wheeler's ``it from bit'' programme~\cite{wheeler2018information}. Where Wheeler's proposal remained largely conceptual, discrete acts of information acquisition giving rise to physical law, we identify an explicit dynamical mechanism: each bit acquired by a physical observer is backed by a measurement-induced energy-momentum transfer, in our example, modelled geometrically as an AS pp-wave, whose gravitational imprint incrementally builds spacetime structure. The bit does not merely describe the geometry; it produces it.

\section{Motivating Heuristics}
\label{sec:heuristics}

Landauer's principle associates a minimal energy cost with the acquisition of information \cite{landauer1961irreversibility}. For an accelerated observer, the relevant temperature is the Unruh temperature \cite{unruh1976notes}. Substituting one into the other eliminates temperature entirely, yielding a relation between information acquisition, energy transfer, and acceleration (a version of the BHT first law, equation (\ref{eq:UL})). This is not merely an algebraic convenience: it is a concrete dictionary between the thermal description natural to a Rindler observer and the non-thermal global Minkowski description, with the apparent thermality of the vacuum emerging as a consequence of the observer's acceleration rather than as a fundamental property of the state. In this way we derive a sequence of standard results in black hole thermodynamics as a consequence of Newton's laws.

\subsection{Momentum Transfer and Acceleration}
In what follows, standard results emerge as consequences of this single relation and Newton's laws. The energy transfer $\Delta E$ implied by equation~\eqref{eq:UL} is carried by a physical quantum — matter or radiation — mediating the measurement interaction. We refer to this as the \emph{thrust quantum} of effective mass $m_t$. (This will actually be a pure energy carrier in our later microstate example.)
For a relativistic carrier, the associated momentum transfer is
\begin{equation}
\Delta p = \frac{\Delta E}{c}.
\end{equation}

A measurement event localized to a spatial scale $r$ is bounded 
by a causal diamond: the intersection of the future light cone of 
its initiation and the past light cone of its completion. The 
temporal extent of this diamond is $\Delta t = 2r/c$, fixed entirely 
by the causal geometry of the event rather than by any dynamical 
assumption. We identify the interaction time with this natural causal 
duration, so that
\begin{equation}
a = \frac{\Delta p}{m_d \Delta t} = \frac{\Delta E}{2 m_d r}.
\label{eq:accel}
\end{equation}

This perspective is closely related in spirit to Erik Verlinde’s entropic gravity \cite{entropic}, where forces arise from entropy gradients, but differs in that the present construction grounds each entropy increment in a discrete measurement event with an associated energy-momentum transfer, rather than in a coarse-grained thermodynamic gradient.

\subsection{The Schwarzschild Radius}
We match the acceleration inferred from the measurement-induced impulse to the Newtonian gravitational field in order to identify the characteristic length scale associated with the detector. The thrust quantum carries energy $\Delta E$ and produces an acceleration $a$ on the detector. Identifying this with the Newtonian gravitational acceleration due to the mass $m_t$ at distance $r$.
\begin{equation}
a = \frac{G m_t}{r^2},
\end{equation}
and substituting equation ~\eqref{eq:accel} with $\Delta E=m_t c^2$, we obtain
\begin{equation}
\frac{m_t c^2}{2 m_d r} = \frac{G m_t}{r^2}.
\end{equation}
Solving for $r$:
\begin{equation}
r = \frac{2 G m_d}{c^2} \equiv r_s,
\end{equation}
which is precisely the Schwarzschild radius of the detector mass $m_d$.

It is notable that the resulting length scale is set by the detector mass $m_d$, rather than the source mass $m_t$. This reflects the fact that the construction is inherently observer-centred: the acceleration entering the argument is the response of the detector to the measurement-induced impulse, and the associated scale is therefore the horizon radius relevant to that observer. In this sense, the quantity $r_s$  should be interpreted not as the Schwarzschild radius of the source, but as the characteristic horizon scale associated with the detector as a physical system undergoing measurement. This is consistent with the observer-dependence of horizon structure in relativistic settings, as exemplified by the Unruh effect, where acceleration alone gives rise to a causal horizon.

\subsection{Surface Gravity}
Evaluating the acceleration at $r = r_s$ gives the surface gravity of the detector:
\begin{equation}
\kappa = \frac{G m_d}{r_s^2} = \frac{c^4}{4 G m_d},
\end{equation}
in agreement with the standard Schwarzschild result. This is the acceleration that appears in the Unruh temperature associated with the detector's horizon, $T = \hbar \kappa / 2\pi k_B c$, providing an internal consistency check on the framework.

\subsection{Bekenstein-Hawking Entropy}
Returning to the Unruh-Landauer relation~\eqref{eq:UL} and substituting the surface gravity $\kappa$ for $a$, the entropy increment associated with an energy increment $\Delta E = c^2 \Delta m_d$ is
\begin{equation}
\Delta H \leq \frac{2\pi c}{\hbar \kappa} \Delta E = \frac{8\pi G m_d}{\hbar c^3} \cdot c^2 \Delta m_d.
\end{equation}
Expressing this in terms of the change in Schwarzschild area $\Delta A = 32\pi G^2 m_d \Delta m_d / c^4$:
\begin{equation}
\Delta H \le \frac{\Delta A}{4 \ell_P^2}, \qquad \ell_P^2 = \frac{\hbar G}{c^3},
\end{equation}
which is the Bekenstein-Hawking area law. Each measurement event contributes a discrete entropy increment proportional to the area imprinted on the horizon by the associated energy-momentum transfer.

This derivation parallels Jacob Bekenstein’s original argument \cite{bekenstein}, in which the entropy increase of a black hole is inferred from the minimal energy required to localize and drop a system across the horizon; here, the same structure emerges from the minimal energy cost of registering information at the horizon scale.

The two bounds are saturated simultaneously when $a = c^2/R$ — which is exactly the condition for being {\it at a horizon}. The horizon is therefore not just where thermodynamics happens to work out, it is the unique locus where the minimum cost of information acquisition equals the maximum information content of the region.

\subsection{Summary}
Beginning from the single relation~\eqref{eq:UL}, and using only Newton's laws and the equivalence principle, we have recovered the Schwarzschild radius, the surface gravity, and the Bekenstein-Hawking area law as consequences of the observation $\rightarrow$ acceleration identification. These are standard results, but their derivation here from the physical cost of information acquisition rather than from thermodynamic postulates is the point.

The thrust quantum introduced in this section, the physical carrier of the measurement-induced impulse, will be given a explicit microstate realization in the sections that follow. It is identified with one of a pair of entangled photons modeled as Aichelburg-Sexl pp-waves crossing past Rindler horizons in the Unruh picture. In this case the back reaction of a single force carrier in one wedge is correlated with an equal and opposite reaction in the other from a common source.

\section{Bilocal Microstate Construction}
\label{sec:microstates}

Let $\hbar$ = $c$ = 1.

The heuristic arguments of Section~\ref{sec:heuristics} establish a relationship between information acquisition, acceleration, and gravitational effects, but leave open the question of what a single microstate looks like concretely. In standard treatments, the Unruh effect is derived by coupling a detector to a pre-existing acceleration and reading off a thermal response from the vacuum. Following~\cite{player2025driving}, we invert this logic: we place an interaction in the past, where a source injects non-thermal entangled excitations into the field, and show that the observer's accelerated response can be driven by these source-induced excitations. The thermal ensemble then emerges as a coarse-grained aggregate of such microstates, rather than being a primitive property of the vacuum.

Each microstate, equation \eqref{eq:microstate_vec} upcomming, is individually 
non-thermal: it carries a definite helicity pair and a definite 
Rindler frequency $\Omega$, neither of which is fixed by the 
Unruh temperature. Thermality is not a property of any single 
microstate but of the ensemble; it emerges when one asks only 
which wedge a detection occurred in, discarding the specific 
microstate identity, and is a consequence of coarse-graining

Landauer gap left open in Section~\ref{sec:heuristics}: in each wedge, the minimum actionable event in the
construction is precisely a single photon, and its polarization state constitutes the
classical bit acquired by each observer from detection. The anticorrelated helicities of the entangled pair
are the natural completion of the $\mathbb{Z}_2$ swap symmetry between wedges, and as we
discuss in Section~\ref{sec:ppwave}, they imprint a definite geometric signature onto the

\subsection{Double-Null Gauge}
%------------------------------------------------------

The emission event naturally defines two null directions: the right-moving photon has
wave-vector $k^\mu_R$ and the left-moving photon has wave-vector $k^\mu_L = -k^\mu_R$. These
two null directions are precisely the $z^+$ and $z^-$ axes of double-null coordinates
\begin{equation}
    z^\pm = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(t \pm z),
\end{equation}
with transverse coordinates $\mathbf{z}_\perp = (z^1, z^2)$. Working in this frame we impose
the symmetric double-null gauge condition
\begin{equation}
    A_+(z) = 0, \qquad A_-(z) = 0,
\label{eq:doublenull}
\end{equation}
so that the gauge field is supported entirely on the transverse components $\mathbf{A}_\perp = (A^1, A^2)$. This gauge respects the $\mathbb{Z}_2$ symmetry that swaps the two wedges $(z^+ \leftrightarrow z^-)$ simultaneously with $(R \leftrightarrow L)$, and therefore treats both observers symmetrically by construction. The residual gauge freedom is fixed by requiring $\partial_i A^i = 0$ in the transverse plane, leaving precisely the two physical helicity degrees of freedom $\epsilon^i_{(\lambda)}$ with $\lambda = \pm 1$; consistency with 
Maxwell's equations and the absence of over-determination are 
verified in Appendix~\ref{gauge_consistency}.

The lack of manifest four-dimensional covariance in equation \eqref{eq:doublenull} is a consequence of aligning the gauge with the pair of physical null directions selected by the source, rather than an arbitrary choice. The full Lorentz group is broken to the little group preserving the pair $\{k^\mu_R, k^\mu_L\}$, which is the two-dimensional Euclidean group $ISO(2)$ acting on the transverse plane. This is precisely the little group of massless particles, and the two helicity eigenstates $\lambda = \pm 1$ are its irreducible representations. All physical results are independent of the gauge choice.

\subsection{The Bilocal Source}
%------------------------------------------------------

In double-null gauge the electromagnetic field is characterised by its transverse components
alone. The bilocal driving source generalises the scalar construction to
\begin{equation}
    J^{ij}(x,y) = \lambda\; \epsilon^{i*}_{(-1)}\, \epsilon^{j}_{(+1)}\; f_L(x)\, f_R(y),
\label{eq:bilocal_vec}
\end{equation}
where $\lambda$ is a coupling constant, $f_L$ and $f_R$ are normalised mode profiles supported
on the left and right wedges respectively, and $\epsilon^i_{(\lambda)}$ are the circular
polarization vectors
\begin{equation}
    \epsilon^i_{(+1)} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{pmatrix}1\\i\end{pmatrix}, \qquad
    \epsilon^i_{(-1)} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{pmatrix}1\\-i\end{pmatrix}.
\end{equation}
The index structure of~\eqref{eq:bilocal_vec} assigns helicity $+1$ to the right-moving mode
and helicity $-1$ to the left-moving mode: the pair is \emph{anticorrelated} in helicity. This
is the unique assignment consistent with angular momentum conservation at the emission vertex
and with the $\mathbb{Z}_2$ wedge-swap symmetry, under which $\epsilon^i_{(+1)} \leftrightarrow
\epsilon^i_{(-1)}$.

The sourced Lagrangian reads
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
    \mathscr{L}_{\text{sourced}}
    &= \mathscr{L}_{\text{free}}
      + \tfrac{1}{2}\lambda \int
          J^{ij}(x,y)\; A^+_i(x)\, A^+_j(y)\; d^4x\, d^4y
      \;+\; \text{h.c.} \\
    &= \mathscr{L}_{\text{free}}
      + \tfrac{1}{2}\lambda
        \Bigl(
          a^{L\dagger}_{-k,(-1)}\, a^{R\dagger}_{k,(+1)}
          \;+\;
          a^{L}_{-k,(-1)}\, a^{R}_{k,(+1)}
        \Bigr),
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:bilocal_lag}
\end{equation}
where $a^{L\dagger}_{-k,(-1)}$ and $a^{R\dagger}_{k,(+1)}$ are creation operators for left-wedge helicity $-1$ and right-wedge helicity $+1$ modes respectively. The interaction term in~\eqref{eq:bilocal_lag} is a two-mode squeezing interaction at frequency $\omega$, now resolved by helicity: it is the direct vector-field analogue of the scalar squeezing interaction~\cite{gerry2023introductory}, with the anticorrelated polarization structure made explicit. The photon pair carries total angular momentum zero, consistent with emission from a scalar source at $\mathcal{P}$.

Each photon carries energy $E = \hbar\omega$ and, as a mode of the electromagnetic field, constitutes precisely one quantum of action $\hbar$ per oscillation cycle. The thrust quantum of Section~\ref{sec:heuristics} is thereby identified with a single photon: the minimum actionable event is the emission and detection of one such quantum.

The Unruh--Landauer bound is saturated not at the level of an individual 
photon but at the level of the source rate. The acceleration $a$ does not 
fix the frequency $\omega$ of each emitted quantum; rather, it determines 
the rate $\dot{N}$ at which the bilocal source must produce entangled pairs 
in order to fully\footnote{The bilocal source may be only part of the acceleration, so $\dot{N}$ is actually a lower bound.} implement that acceleration, with the total power 
$\dot{N}\hbar\omega$ matching the Unruh thermal flux. Each individual photon 
carries exactly one quantum of action $\hbar$ and one binary helicity 
outcome, one bit in the sense of one binary alternative, carrying 
$\ln 2$ nats, and these are paired by the construction independently 
of $a$. The Landauer bound then operates as a consistency condition on 
the ensemble: the minimum energy cost per bit acquired, summed over the 
rate of acquisition, equals the power required to sustain the acceleration.

\subsection{Polarization Bogoliubov Transformation}
%------------------------------------------------------

We now show that the polarization labels survive the Rindler--Unruh mode decomposition,
so that the thermal ensemble retains its helicity structure and the anticorrelation of the
emitted pair descends unambiguously to the level of individual Rindler microstates.

In the transverse plane, the electromagnetic field in double-null gauge decomposes into
two independent scalar-like sectors labelled by helicity $\lambda = \pm 1$. For each sector,
the right-wedge field expands as
\begin{equation}
    A^i_\lambda(z) = \int_0^\infty \frac{d\Omega}{2\pi}\,
        \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\Omega}}\,\epsilon^i_{(\lambda)}
        \left[
            b^R_{\Omega,\lambda}\, u^R_\Omega(z)
            + b^{R\dagger}_{\Omega,\lambda}\, u^{R*}_\Omega(z)
        \right],
\end{equation}
where $u^R_\Omega(z)$ are the right-wedge Rindler modes at Rindler frequency $\Omega$, and
$b^R_{\Omega,\lambda}$, $b^{R\dagger}_{\Omega,\lambda}$ are the corresponding annihilation
and creation operators satisfying
\begin{equation}
    \left[b^R_{\Omega,\lambda},\, b^{R\dagger}_{\Omega',\lambda'}\right]
    = \delta(\Omega - \Omega')\,\delta_{\lambda\lambda'}.
\end{equation}
An identical expansion holds in the left wedge with $R \to L$ and $\lambda \to -\lambda$,
reflecting the anticorrelated helicity assignment. Because each helicity sector is
independently a scalar-like sector, the Bogoliubov transformation relating Minkowski and
Rindler modes takes the same form in each sector. For helicity $\lambda$ at Rindler
frequency $\Omega$, the Unruh modes are
\begin{equation}
    c_{\Omega,\lambda} = \cosh\theta_\Omega\; b^R_{\Omega,\lambda}
                       - \sinh\theta_\Omega\; b^{L\dagger}_{\Omega,-\lambda},
\label{eq:bogoliubov}
\end{equation}
where $\tanh\theta_\Omega = e^{-\pi\Omega c/a}$ encodes the Unruh distribution at acceleration $a$, and $-\lambda$ on the left-wedge operator reflects the anticorrelation. The transformation~\eqref{eq:bogoliubov} is the standard Bogoliubov relation for a massless field~\cite{unruh1976notes, birrell1984quantum}, now carrying a helicity label throughout. The Minkowski vacuum in the Rindler basis reads, for each helicity sector,
\begin{equation}
    |0\rangle_M^{(\lambda)}
    = \frac{1}{\cosh\theta_\Omega}
      \sum_{n=0}^\infty \tanh^n\theta_\Omega\;
      |n_{\Omega,\lambda}\rangle_R\,
      |n_{\Omega,-\lambda}\rangle_L,
\label{eq:vacuum_vec}
\end{equation}
and the full vacuum is the product over all $\Omega$ and both helicities:
\begin{equation}
    |0\rangle_M = \prod_{\Omega,\lambda} |0\rangle_M^{(\lambda)}.
\end{equation}

Equation~\eqref{eq:vacuum_vec} makes explicit that the Minkowski vacuum is a sum over
entangled helicity-anticorrelated pairs at each Rindler frequency. The bilocal source
selects a single term from this sum at fixed $\Omega$ and fixed helicity pair
$(\lambda, -\lambda) = (+1,-1)$, yielding the single-microstate
\begin{equation}
    |\Psi_{\Omega}\rangle_M
    = |1_{\Omega,+1}\rangle_R\,|1_{\Omega,-1}\rangle_L
      + \mathcal{O}(\text{higher order}).
\label{eq:microstate_vec}
\end{equation}
$\mathcal{R}$'s detection of helicity $+1$ and $\mathcal{L}$'s detection of helicity $-1$ are therefore
perfectly anticorrelated: each measurement event yields one classical bit, and the two bits
are the complement of one another.

The key structural result is that the helicity label $\lambda$ is conserved under the
Bogoliubov transformation: it enters equation~\eqref{eq:bogoliubov} as a spectator index and
exits unchanged on the Unruh operators $c_{\Omega,\lambda}$. Thermality, when it arises from
coarse-graining over the ensemble of microstates~\eqref{eq:microstate_vec}, is therefore
helicity-resolved: each helicity sector thermalises independently, and the anticorrelation
between wedges is preserved at every level of the construction.

\section{Matter as Substrate}
\label{sec:substrate}

The Unruh–Landauer relation of Section~\ref{sec:heuristics} constrains the minimal energy required to register information, but does not specify where that information resides physically. The present section addresses this gap by identifying matter itself as the substrate in which such information is stored, with each act of acquisition corresponding to the deposition of a discrete quantum of action into the detector’s degrees of freedom. We show how information is encoded in matter and energy and is reflected in the horizon geometry. This requires a physical account of what it means for matter to \emph{carry} information.

A massive particle accumulates phase along its worldline at a rate set by its rest energy. The Compton frequency relation $f = E/h$ assigns a natural frequency to any mass $m$, and Bremermann's computational limit~\cite{bremermann1962optimization} independently recovers the same rate $f = mc^2/h$ as the maximum clock rate, or Nyquist rate, of any physical system with that energy $E$. These two results agree because they are both expressions of the same underlying fact: a quantum of action $\hbar$ is the minimal unit of physical evolution, and $mc^2/h$ is the rate at which a mass $m$ consumes such quanta.

Each such quantum corresponds to one sample\footnote{We use \emph{sample} throughout to denote a single zero crossing of the phase $e^{iS/\hbar}$; readers may equivalently read this as \emph{event} or \emph{tick}. It is only defined upto the Heisenberg resolution.} of the phase $e^{iS/\hbar}$ in the path integral, and classical trajectories emerge from the constructive interference of these samples across paths. Paths separated by less than one sample of action, less than $\hbar$, do 
not produce resolvable interference fringes: the phase difference is 
insufficient to complete a full cycle of constructive and destructive 
interference, so the trajectory cannot be distinguished at sub-sample 
resolution. The trajectory is resolved only down to the scale set by 
$\hbar/E$, which is precisely the Heisenberg uncertainty at energy $E$.
This corresponds to a natural ultraviolet cutoff given by the energy $E$ of the system itself. Higher energies only come into play when external systems interact with the energy $E$ system.

The distinction between massless and massive degrees of freedom is therefore operational: massless quanta transmit information, while massive systems retain it, accumulating the action quanta that constitute physical memory. A massless particle, the photon, has no rest frame, no proper time, and accumulates no action between emission and absorption. It does not sample. It therefore carries no persistent
memory and cannot serve as a substrate. Its role in the present construction is precisely
that of a pure information carrier. A single photon carries exactly two internal irreducible constituents, distinct in kind:

\begin{itemize}

\item \textbf{One quantum of action $\hbar$}: the physical transfer, 
deposited as energy $\hbar\omega$ into the detector, incrementing its 
Bremermann sample rate by $\Delta f = \hbar\omega/h$ for as long as the 
energy remains in the mass. This increment is the physical trace of the 
interaction in the detector's matter-memory.
\item \textbf{One bit of polarization}: the informational outcome, the 
classical record of which helicity, $\lambda = +1$ or $\lambda = -1$ 
--- was absorbed. This bit persists in the detector's state for as long 
as the detector retains the record. Erasure of this bit carries a minimum 
energy cost governed by Landauer's principle, regardless of how the 
record was originally established.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Types of sample}

From a given observer’s perspective, some samples appear to carry information while others do not. One can describe three types of samples in terms of how the observer registers or retains their outcomes.

\begin{itemize}
\item {\bf Absorption samples} are measurement events: a photon is 
detected, one quantum of action $\hbar$ is deposited persistently into the 
detector's matter-memory, and one helicity outcome is written as a classical 
bit. The Landauer cost $\Delta E \geq k_B T \ln 2$ is paid immediately and 
in full on acquisition. This is the sample category that drives the bilocal 
construction: $\mathcal{R}$ and $\mathcal{L}$ each undergo exactly one absorption sample per 
emitted pair, and it is these samples whose gravitational imprint is modelled 
by the AS pp-wave in Section~\ref{sec:ppwave}.

\item {\bf Free samples} are pure phase accumulation events: a massive particle 
propagates along its worldline, incrementing its sample count at the Bremermann 
rate $mc^2/h$, without writing any bit into any detector's state. No Landauer 
cost is incurred, no classical record is produced, and no observer acquires 
information. These are the samples that constitute mass and proper time in the 
background sense, the substrate on which absorption and emission events act.

\item {\bf Emission samples} are the time-reverse of absorption: a bit stored in 
matter-memory is released, the corresponding quantum of action is re-emitted 
as radiation, and the Landauer cost is paid on the way \emph{out} rather than 
on the way in. This is the category Landauer's principle most directly 
governs in its original erasure formulation.
\end{itemize}

A moving mirror realizes absorption and emission as reflection: it
receives a photon, stores the helicity bit transiently in its 
matter degrees of freedom during a period of free sample accumulation, and 
re-emits the photon, paying the erasure cost at emission. An ensemble of 
such mirrors, say two silvered mirrors  back to back, can cycle through absorption, free accumulation, and 
emission without ever producing a permanent classical record.

It is worth noting that the absorption/free/emission classification 
is observer-relative in the same sense as quantum state reduction 
itself. What constitutes an irreversible absorption sample for one 
observer may be a free sample for an external observer. If $\mathcal{R}$ truly forgets the helicity 
outcome, in the sense that his matter-memory becomes disentangled 
from the bit, the photon can interfere with its partner at a 
future $\mathcal{P}$. The classification is not a property of the interaction 
itself but of the observer's epistemic access to the outcome. True 
irreversibility is approached asymptotically as the bit becomes 
correlated with an increasing number of environmental degrees of 
freedom, which is the standard decoherence picture now applied to 
the sample classification.

The $\hbar$ is what makes the sample physical; the bit is what 
makes it meaningful to an observer. These are distinct: the action 
quantum is deposited regardless of whether a persistent record is 
produced, while the bit exists only relative to an observer for 
whom the outcome is accessible. That a single photon carries one 
of each in the minimal case is the reason it is the natural unit 
of the construction.

\subsection{Penrose's Zig-Zag Mechanism}
\label{sec:zigzag}

The matter substrate argument of the preceding subsection established that massive particles
sample at the Bremermann rate $mc^2/h$ and that each sample is a quantum of action $\hbar$
accumulated in the path integral phase $e^{iS/\hbar}$. But it left open a prior question:
\emph{why} does matter sample at all? The zig-zag\footnote{Penrose calls these particle {\it zig-zag} particles, we are tempted to call them {\it tick-tock} particles, but only in a footnote.} construction of Penrose and Dirac supplies
a concrete physical answer, and in doing so reveals that the bilocal microstate construction
of Section~\ref{sec:microstates} is not merely analogous to the origin of mass, it is
structurally identical to it at the level of individual events.

\subsubsection{The Dirac 4-Spinor as a Bilocal System}

The Lorentz algebra decomposes as
\begin{equation}
    \mathfrak{so}(3,1) \cong \mathfrak{su}(2) \times \mathfrak{su}(2),
\end{equation}
where the two $\mathfrak{su}(2)$ factors correspond to the two helicities: the first is
generated by boosts combined with counter-clockwise rotations, and the second by boosts
combined with clockwise rotations~\cite{ryder1996quantum}. A Dirac 4-spinor decomposes under this
algebra into two Weyl 2-spinors of opposite helicity $\lambda = \pm\frac{1}{2}$ and
equal and opposite momenta. Penrose~\cite{penrose2006road} proposes a physical interpretation
of this decomposition: the two 2-spinors are real physical objects, each moving
instantaneously at the speed of light in opposite directions, interacting at regular
intervals via the Higgs field at the De Broglie frequency.

The structural parallel with the bilocal construction is very close:

\begin{itemize}
    \item The two Weyl 2-spinors, moving at $c$ in opposite directions with opposite
    helicity, correspond to the two photons emitted by $\mathcal{P}$ into the left and right
    Rindler wedges.
    \item The Higgs field plays the role of $\mathcal{R}$ and $\mathcal{L}$: it is the common mediator whose
    interaction reflects the entangled pair and sets the interaction rate.
    \item Between Higgs interactions, each 2-spinor is massless and moves at $c$,
    carrying no persistent memory, pure information carriers, exactly as the photons
    in the bilocal construction.
    \item The Higgs interaction event itself is the measurement event: it deposits one
    quantum of action $\hbar$, flips the active helicity from $+\frac{1}{2}$ to
    $-\frac{1}{2}$ and back, and produces one sample of proper time.
\end{itemize}

Mass emerges from the regularity of these interactions: a particle is massive because it
undergoes repeated Higgs interactions at the De Broglie frequency, and its mass is
proportional to that frequency via $E = \hbar\omega$. The Bremermann sample rate $mc^2/h$
is therefore not a coincidence or a heuristic, it is the Higgs interaction rate,
the rate at which the zig-zag mechanism deposits quanta of action into the particle's
proper time.

\subsubsection{Mass as Accumulated Measurement History}

Between Higgs interactions, the two 2-spinors are massless and moving at $c$ in opposite
null directions. Each spinor therefore has a local Rindler structure relative to the other:
from the perspective of the right-moving spinor, the left-moving spinor is behind a causal
horizon, and vice versa. The Higgs interaction is the event that collapses this local
horizon and produces one sample, one unit of proper time, exactly as the photon
absorption by $\mathcal{R}$ collapses the Rindler horizon and produces one sample in the bilocal
construction.

This gives a precise sense in which mass is an accumulated measurement history. Each Higgs
interaction is a measurement event: it deposits
one quantum of action $\hbar$ into the particle's matter-memory, persistently incrementing
its sample rate by $\Delta f = \Delta E/h$. The particle's mass is the total accumulated
record of all such interactions since its formation. Matter is not just the substrate for information; its mass encodes the accumulated history of its Higgs interaction events.


\subsubsection{Proper Time as a Foliation of Micro-Horizons}

The series of Higgs interaction events along a particle's worldline is a one-dimensional
foliation of the proper time line by discrete events, each of which is a micro-horizon

one-dimensional analogue of the Rindler horizon separating left and right wedges in the
bilocal construction, and it matches the holographic screen picture of
Verlinde~\cite{entropic}: each screen is a surface at which information is registered and

\subsection{Matter as a Physical Channel}
\label{sec:channel}

The sampling rate and dynamic range of matter can be combined into 
a channel capacity in the Shannon sense. For a system of mass $m$ 
with internal Hilbert space dimension $d$, the maximum rate of 
information flow is

\begin{equation}
    C = \underbrace{\frac{mc^2}{h}}_{\text{bandwidth}} 
        \times 
        \underbrace{\log_2(d)}_{\text{dynamic range}}
        \quad \text{(bits per second),}
    \label{eq:channel_capacity}
\end{equation}
where the bandwidth is the Bremermann sampling rate and the dynamic 
range is the information content per sample. The product 
$C$ is the \emph{channel capacity} of the matter substrate: the 
maximum rate at which physical interactions can write information 
into its degrees of freedom.

The three sample types of Section~\ref{sec:substrate} correspond 
to three distinct regimes of the data processing inequality 
$H(\text{out}) \leq H(\text{in})$, which states that information 
cannot be created by physical processes, only preserved or lost:

\begin{equation}
\text{Free:} \quad H(\text{out}) = H(\text{in}), 
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\text{Absorption:} \quad H(\text{field, out}) < H(\text{field, in}),
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\text{Emission:} \quad H(\text{detector, out}) < H(\text{detector, in}).
\end{equation}

Free samples saturate the inequality: no information is lost or 
gained, the channel runs at capacity without transmitting anything. 
Absorption samples strictly decrease the field's information content 
as it flows into the detector: the channel is being \emph{used}, 
information is being written into matter-memory at a rate bounded 
by $C$. Emission samples strictly decrease the detector's information 
content as the stored bit is released back into the field: the 
channel runs in reverse, and the Landauer cost is the energy 
price of that reversal.

The data processing inequality is therefore the information-theoretic 
statement of the thermodynamic arrow of time in this framework: 
absorption and emission are the two directions of persistent 
information flow, and free samples are the reversible background 
against which both are measured. The full quantum channel structure 
of the Bogoliubov transformation, its classical capacity in the 
Holevo--Schumacher--Westmoreland, we leave for future work.

The Bekenstein and Bremermann bounds are therefore the same 
constraint viewed from orthogonal directions: Bekenstein counts 
information capacity spatially, as area in Planck units, while 
Bremermann counts it temporally, as samples per second. 
Integrating the channel capacity over the natural causal 
timescale $\tau \sim Gm/c^3$ gives
\begin{equation}
    C \cdot \tau = \frac{mc^2}{h} \cdot \frac{Gm}{c^3} 
    = \frac{Gm^2}{hc} \sim H_{\mathrm{Bekenstein}},
\end{equation}
up to numerical factors and the dynamic range $\log_2(d)$. 
The spacelike and timelike information bounds are 
therefore consistent faces of the same underlying constraint.

\section{pp-Wave Geometry}
\label{sec:ppwave}
WLOG we introduce coordinates for the photon traveling from $\mathcal{P}$ $(-1,0)$ to $\mathcal{R}$ $(0,1)$. Let $u = t - z + 1$ and $v = t + z + 1$, where the photon propagates along the null plane $u = 0$, $z$ is the longitudinal direction, and $(x,y)$ are transverse coordinates. Then $(u,v)$ = $(0,0)$ is $\mathcal{P}$'s location, $(u,v)$ = $(0,2)$ is $\mathcal{R}$'s location, and $(u,v)$ = $(0,1)$ is the intersection of the photon with the past right Rinder horizon. The situtation is reversed for the left moving photon, we swap $u$ and $v$ to describe photon moving from $\mathcal{P}$ to $\mathcal{L}$.

\subsection{The AS pp-Wave and Its Effects}

Test particles at fixed transverse positions $(x,y)$ experience two effects as the shock front crosses $u = 0$:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Longitudinal shift.} The $v$ coordinate is displaced by
    \begin{equation}
    \Delta v \propto -\ln r^2, \qquad r^2 = x^2 + y^2.
    \end{equation}
    This shift is lightlike: no proper time elapses for a particle crossing the shock, but it is displaced along the null direction. Near the axis ($r \to 0$) the shift diverges, encoding the concentration of energy along the photon's trajectory.

    \item \textbf{Transverse focusing.} The gradient of the longitudinal shift produces a transverse velocity
    \begin{equation}
    \Delta \mathbf{v}_\perp \propto \nabla \ln r \propto \frac{1}{r},
    \end{equation}
    directed toward the axis. Test particles are pulled toward the centre of the wave, regardless of their initial transverse separation.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{The Longitudinal Shift Makes Space}

The photon emitted by $\mathcal{P}$ propagates along the null direction; as it crosses the Rindler horizon, the AS shift displaces the future light cone of every test particle it passes. For the photon traveling into the right wedge, the shift pushes the right wedge's geometry in the $+v$ direction; for the entangled partner traveling into the left wedge, the shift pushes the left wedge in the $-v$ direction. The two wedges are displaced \emph{away from each other} along the longitudinal null direction. See Figure \ref{paz_shift}.

\subsection{Transverse Focusing Increments Horizon Area}
\label{sec:deltaA}

The transverse focusing of the AS pp-wave produces a calculable 
increment in horizon area. We derive this using the Raychaudhuri 
equation on the null congruence generating the Rindler horizon, 
following Jacobson~\cite{jacobson1995thermodynamics}, but applied 
here to a single microstate rather than a thermodynamic average.

For a null congruence with generators $k^\mu$ and expansion 
$\theta$, the Raychaudhuri equation gives
\begin{equation}
\frac{d\theta}{du} = -\tfrac{1}{2}\theta^2 
    - \sigma_{\mu\nu}\sigma^{\mu\nu} 
    - 8\pi G\, T_{\mu\nu} k^\mu k^\nu.
\end{equation}
Integrating over the horizon patch in the linearised regime 
and using $k^\mu = \delta^\mu_u$, the area change is
\begin{equation}
\Delta A = 8\pi G \int T_{uu}\, du\, d^2x_\perp.
\label{eq:deltaA}
\end{equation}
In a strict AS construction, the source is a point particle 
and $T_{\mu\nu}$ is distributional, concentrated on the axis. 
The present construction provides a natural regularization: the 
photon is a normalizable mode of the electromagnetic field in 
double-null gauge, with a smooth transverse profile set by the 
wavelength $\sim c/\omega$. The electromagnetic stress-energy 
$T_{uu}$ is well-defined throughout, integrates to $\hbar\omega$ 
over the transverse plane by energy conservation, and recovers 
the strict AS limit as $\omega \to \infty$ with the logarithmic 
divergence regulated at the scale $c/\omega$.

Substituting into equation~\eqref{eq:deltaA} gives
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\Delta A = 8\pi G\,\hbar\omega = 8\pi G\,\Delta E.}
\label{eq:deltaA_result}
\end{equation}
The horizon area grows by one discrete, calculable increment 
per photon absorbed, microstate by microstate, without 
reference to bulk thermodynamic averages.

\end{equation}

\subsection{Action and the Gravitational Boundary}
\label{sec:microstate_action}

Each absorption sample deposits one quantum of action $\hbar$ into 
the detector's matter-memory. The total action accumulated across 
all measurement events is therefore
%
\begin{equation}
S_{\text{matter}} = \sum_i \hbar\omega_i,
\end{equation}
%
where the sum is over all absorption samples, each contributing 
energy $\hbar\omega_i$ to the detector mass. This is the 
matter action counted sample by sample: one quantum of action 
per event, one event per emitted pair.

The same events have a geometric description. By 
eq.~\eqref{eq:deltaA_result}, each absorption sample contributes 
a horizon area increment $\Delta A_i = 8\pi G\hbar\omega_i$, so 
the total area increment summed over all events is
%
\begin{equation}
\sum_i \Delta A_i = 8\pi G \sum_i \hbar\omega_i = 8\pi G\, 
S_{\text{matter}}.
\end{equation}
%
This geometric sum has a precise identification in the 
gravitational action. On a stationary Rindler horizon the 
unperturbed expansion of the null generators vanishes, $\theta = 
0$, so the Gibbons--Hawking--York boundary 
term~\cite{gibbons1977action} is sourced entirely by the AS 
pp-wave perturbations:
%
\begin{equation}
S_{GHY}\big|_{\mathcal{H}} 
= \frac{c^4}{8\pi G}\int_{\mathcal{H}} \theta\, d^2x_\perp\, du 
= \frac{c^4}{8\pi G}\sum_i \Delta A_i 
= \frac{c^4}{8\pi G} \cdot 8\pi G\sum_i \hbar\omega_i 
= \sum_i \hbar\omega_i
= S_{\text{matter}}.
\label{eq:GHY_micro}
\end{equation}
%
The matter action and the gravitational boundary action are 
therefore equal, event by event, on the stationary Rindler 
horizon. This is not a bulk result: the bulk Einstein--Hilbert 
action vanishes on-shell, and it is the boundary term that 
carries the physical content. The microstate construction 
identifies what that boundary content is: each contribution to 
$S_{GHY}$ corresponds to one quantum of action deposited into 
matter-memory by one measurement event.

This result is consistent with and extends programmes of 
Jacobson~\cite{jacobson1995thermodynamics} and

Einstein--Hilbert action is the fundamental gravitational degree

thermodynamic averages. The present construction realises the 
same identification at the level of individual microstates, with 
each boundary contribution sourced by a specific quantum 
measurement event rather than a bulk average. The standard 
result emerges from summing over the ensemble; the microstate 
picture supplies what the thermodynamic programmes leave open: 
the physical process underlying each boundary contribution.

\section{Entanglement as Spatial Information}

\end{center}

$\mathcal{L}$ (left wedge) or $\mathcal{R}$ (right wedge).

polarization state but a location in space.

Each measurement event has exactly two
irreducible constituents: one quantum of action $\hbar$ written persistently into the
detector's matter-memory as a series of samples, and one bit of polarization registered as a classical
outcome. The entanglement between $\mathcal{R}$ and $\mathcal{L}$ is consumed in producing these two
records, an increase in sampling rate and one bit at each wedge.
What remains after measurement is not the entanglement itself 
but its classical residue: the perfect anticorrelation between
the two bits, and the correlated pair of samples written into the matter on each side of the
horizon. The spatial bit is precisely the polarization bit 
now read as a location rather than a spin state: the same bit that records \emph{what} was
observed also records \emph{where}, because in this construction the two wedges carry
opposite helicities by design. This double duty is not merely convenient but forced: the 
anticorrelated helicity assignment of the bilocal source makes 
the two wedges distinguishable by construction, so the spatial 
bit is already latent in the source geometry before any 
measurement occurs.

The information is carried by the matter of the detectors, not by the horizon. As
established in Section~\ref{sec:substrate}, the horizon is a boundary that makes the
left/right distinction sharp; it is the matter on each side that accumulates the samples and
holds the classical record. $\mathcal{R}$'s detector has absorbed one photon, increased its sampling rate, and registered polarization $H_+/H_-$. $\mathcal{L}$'s detector has absorbed one photon, increased its sampling rate, and registered polarization $H_-/H_+$. The two records are perfectly complementary by the anticorrelated structure of the bilocal source, and their complementarity is encoded in matter before it
is reflected in geometry.

future work.

\section{Carnot Cycle from Unruh--Landauer}

It is this irreversibility that allows us to construct a example of a Carnot cycle at the level of microstates. \begin{itemize}

\end{itemize}


\subsection{Shannon Entropy}

The entropy variation in the isothermal legs should be understood as Shannon entropy associated with measurement outcomes in a fixed operational basis, rather than the von Neumann entropy of the global quantum state, which remains pure throughout. From an information-theoretic perspective aligned with Copenhagen interpretation and QBism, the quantum state encodes an observer’s knowledge rather than an objective microstate, and thermodynamic entropy should therefore be identified with the classical uncertainty in measurement outcomes. In this sense, Shannon entropy provides the operationally relevant notion entering Landauer's principle, resolving the apparent tension between unitary evolution (which preserves von Neumann entropy) and thermodynamic irreversibility (which is manifest at the level of classical information processing).

No decoherence assumption is required; Shannon entropy here is defined over the classical outcome distribution induced by a fixed measurement basis (the Rindler detector algebra), independent of the unitary evolution of the global state.

\begin{equation}
V_{\text{eff}} \equiv \log_2 \dim\!\left(\mathcal{H}_{\text{rev}}\right),
\end{equation}

In this sense, the thermodynamic ``volume'' is identified with the size of the reversible entanglement channel between $\mathcal{H}_L$ and $\mathcal{H}_R$, rather than with the total Hilbert space itself. Expansion corresponds to an increase in reversible entanglement accessibility, while compression corresponds to its partial conversion into matter-memory.

\section{Appendix: Consistency of the Double-Null Gauge Condition}
\label{gauge_consistency}
% Verification that double-null gauge does not over-constrain physical degrees of freedom

We verify that the double-null gauge condition $A_+ = A_- = 0$ is consistent with 
Maxwell's equations and leaves exactly two physical degrees of freedom.

\subsection{Coordinates and Notation}

In double-null coordinates $(x^+, x^-, x^1, x^2)$ the Minkowski metric reads
\begin{equation}
    ds^2 = -2\,dx^+\,dx^- + (dx^1)^2 + (dx^2)^2,
\end{equation}
so that $\partial_+ = \partial/\partial x^+$, $\partial_- = \partial/\partial x^-$, and 
the d'Alembertian is
\begin{equation}
    \Box = -2\partial_+\partial_- + \partial_i\partial^i, \qquad i = 1,2.
\end{equation}
The gauge field has components $A_\mu = (A_+, A_-, A_1, A_2)$ and transforms as
\begin{equation}
    A_\mu \to A_\mu + \partial_\mu\Lambda
\end{equation}
under a gauge transformation with parameter $\Lambda(x^+, x^-, x^1, x^2)$.

\subsection{Imposing the Gauge Conditions in Stages}

The single gauge parameter $\Lambda$ is a function of all four coordinates. We use 
it in three stages, each consuming a different functional dependence.

\paragraph{Stage 1: $A_+ = 0$.}
Requiring $A_+ + \partial_+\Lambda = 0$ determines $\Lambda$ as a function of $x^+$:
\begin{equation}
    \Lambda(x^+, x^-, x^i) = -\int^{x^+} A_+(s, x^-, x^i)\,ds 
                              + \Lambda_0(x^-, x^i),
\end{equation}
where $\Lambda_0(x^-, x^i)$ is an arbitrary residual function independent of $x^+$.

\paragraph{Stage 2: $A_- = 0$.}
Under the residual freedom $\Lambda_0(x^-, x^i)$, the $A_-$ component transforms as
$A_- \to A_- + \partial_-\Lambda_0$. Setting this to zero determines $\Lambda_0$ as a 
function of $x^-$:
\begin{equation}
    \Lambda_0(x^-, x^i) = -\int^{x^-} A_-(s, x^i)\,ds + \Lambda_1(x^i),
\end{equation}
where $\Lambda_1(x^i)$ is a residual function of the transverse coordinates only.

\paragraph{Stage 3: Transverse Coulomb condition $\partial^i A_i = 0$.}
Under the remaining freedom $\Lambda_1(x^i)$, the transverse components transform as 
$A_i \to A_i + \partial_i\Lambda_1$. Imposing $\partial^i A_i = 0$ requires
\begin{equation}
    \nabla^2_\perp\Lambda_1 = -\partial^i A_i,
\end{equation}
which is a Poisson equation in the transverse plane with a unique solution (up to 
harmonic functions, fixed by boundary conditions). This exhausts the gauge freedom 
entirely.

The three conditions have consumed the full gauge parameter $\Lambda$ in stages, 
with no over-determination: each stage uses a strictly different functional 
dependence of $\Lambda$.

\subsection{Physical Degrees of Freedom}

After imposing $A_+ = A_- = 0$ and $\partial^i A_i = 0$, the remaining components 
are $A_1$ and $A_2$ subject to one differential constraint $\partial^i A_i = 0$. 
In momentum space this reads $k^i A_i = 0$.

For our specific construction, the photons propagate in the $\pm z$ direction, 
so their wave-vectors are purely null: $k^i_\perp = 0$ for $i = 1,2$. The 
transverse Coulomb condition $k^i A_i = 0$ is therefore trivially satisfied for 
any $A_1, A_2$, and both transverse components are independent. The two circular 
polarization vectors
\begin{equation}
    \epsilon^i_{(+1)} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{pmatrix}1\\i\end{pmatrix}, \qquad
    \epsilon^i_{(-1)} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{pmatrix}1\\-i\end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
are the two independent physical degrees of freedom, corresponding to helicity 
$\lambda = \pm 1$. This is the correct count for a massless spin-1 field in four 
dimensions.

The trivial satisfaction of the transverse Coulomb condition 
relies on the photons propagating purely in the $\pm z$ direction 
with vanishing transverse momentum $k^i_\perp = 0$. For photons 
with nonzero transverse momentum the condition $k^i A_i = 0$ 
imposes a non-trivial constraint and the degree of freedom count 
requires revisiting.

\subsection{Consistency with Maxwell's Equations}

With $A_+ = A_- = 0$ and $\partial^i A_i = 0$, the non-zero field strength 
components are
\begin{equation}
    F_{+i} = \partial_+ A_i, \qquad F_{-i} = \partial_- A_i, \qquad 
    F_{ij} = \partial_i A_j - \partial_j A_i, \qquad F_{+-} = 0.
\end{equation}
Maxwell's equations $\partial^\mu F_{\mu\nu} = J_\nu$ decompose as follows.

\paragraph{Transverse components $\nu = i$:}
\begin{equation}
    -2\partial_+\partial_- A_i + \partial^j(\partial_j A_i - \partial_i A_j) = J_i.
\end{equation}
Using $\partial^i A_i = 0$, the second term simplifies to $\partial^j\partial_j A_i$, 
giving the standard wave equation
\begin{equation}
    \boxed{\Box A_i = J_i.}
    \label{eq:wave}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Null components $\nu = \pm$:}
\begin{equation}
    -\partial^i\partial_\pm A_i = J_\pm.
\end{equation}
These are constraint equations relating the null source components to the transverse 
field. For our bilocal source, which is purely transverse, $J_+ = J_- = 0$, and the 
constraints reduce to
\begin{equation}
    \partial^i\partial_\pm A_i = 0.
\end{equation}
Differentiating the transverse Coulomb condition $\partial^i A_i = 0$ with respect 
to $x^\pm$ gives $\partial^i(\partial_\pm A_i) = 0$, so these constraints are 
automatically satisfied. \checkmark

\subsection{Summary}

The double-null gauge condition $A_+ = A_- = 0$ supplemented by the transverse 
Coulomb condition $\partial^i A_i = 0$:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item uses the gauge freedom of $\Lambda$ completely and without over-determination,
          via a three-stage procedure consuming the $x^+$-, $x^-$-, and $x^i$-dependent
          parts of $\Lambda$ in sequence;
    \item leaves exactly two physical degrees of freedom, the helicity eigenstates
          $\lambda = \pm 1$, consistent with the known count for a massless spin-1 field;
    \item reduces Maxwell's equations to the standard transverse wave equation
          $\Box A_i = J_i$, with the null constraint equations automatically satisfied
          for a purely transverse source.
\end{enumerate}
The gauge is therefore internally consistent and does not over-constrain the physical 
degrees of freedom. The $\mathbb{Z}_2$ wedge-swap symmetry is preserved throughout: 
conditions on $A_+$ and $A_-$ are imposed symmetrically, and neither null direction 
is privileged over the other.

\section{Conclusion}

We have proposed that quantum measurement is the physical process 
underlying each discrete entropy increment in the thermodynamic 
theory of gravity. Beginning from the single observer-relative 
relation between information acquisition, energy transfer, and 
acceleration, the Unruh--Landauer relation~\eqref{eq:UL}, 
and using only Newton's laws and the equivalence principle, we 
recovered the Schwarzschild radius, surface gravity, and 
Bekenstein--Hawking area law as consequences of the physical cost 
of observation. The saturation of this bound at one bit identifies 
the horizon distance as the natural length scale of a single-bit 
measurement event, with the Rindler horizon as the unique surface 
at which the Landauer bound is geometrically realized.

We grounded these heuristics in a concrete bilocal microstate 
construction, extending the scalar construction 
of~\cite{player2025driving} to the electromagnetic field. A 
source $\mathcal{P}$ in the causal past emits an entangled photon 
pair into the left and right Rindler wedges, where observers 
$\mathcal{L}$ and $\mathcal{R}$ each register one helicity 
outcome and acquire one classical bit. Each photon carries 
exactly one quantum of action $\hbar$ and one binary helicity 
outcome, and the anticorrelated helicity structure is conserved 
through the Bogoliubov transformation, so the thermal ensemble 
retains its helicity structure at every level of the 
construction. Thermality is not a primitive but an emergent 
property of the coarse-grained ensemble of individually 
non-thermal microstates.

The central geometric result is that each absorption event 
imprints a discrete, calculable increment on the horizon area:
\begin{equation}
\Delta A = 8\pi G\,\hbar\omega,
\end{equation}
derived bottom-up from the matter sample picture and
the Raychaudhuri equation, applied to the AS 
pp-wave stress-energy matching \cite{jacobson1995thermodynamics}. The AS longitudinal shift is a 
supertranslation at the Rindler horizon in the sense of 
Hawking, Perry, and Strominger~\cite{hawking2016soft}, and 
each measurement event excites one mode of the 
$\mathrm{BMS}_3$ algebra whose central charge Carlip 
uses to derive the Bekenstein--Hawking 
entropy~\cite{carlip2018bms}. The physical process that 
Carlip's programme leaves unspecified, the process that 
excites the boundary symmetry modes, is quantum 
measurement.

The matter action and the gravitational boundary action are 
two accountings of the same microstate events:
\begin{equation}
S_{GHY}\big|_{\mathcal{H}} = \sum_i \hbar\omega_i 
= S_{\text{matter}},
\end{equation}
identifying the Gibbons--Hawking--York boundary 
term~\cite{gibbons1977action} as the natural gravitational 
counterpart of the matter action at the microstate level, 
and realizing at the level of individual microstates the 
claim of Jacobson~\cite{jacobson1995thermodynamics} and 
Padmanabhan~\cite{padmanabhan2010thermodynamical} that the 
boundary term is the fundamental gravitational degree of 
freedom.

Entanglement is converted into geometry event by event. The 
EPR side of the construction is the entangled photon pair 
emitted by $\mathcal{P}$; the ER side is the pair of 
correlated AS longitudinal shifts in opposite null 
directions, which together constitute a geometric bridge 
between the two wedges. The spatial bit, the answer to 
``who observed $H_+$?'', is the classical record of which 
side of the bridge each measurement occurred on. Matter is 
the substrate in which this record persists: the horizon is 
a boundary that makes the left/right distinction sharp, but 
it is the matter on each side that accumulates the samples 
and holds the classical record. The bridge is built from 
matter, sample by sample.

Several directions remain open. The Carnot cycle extension,
in which $\mathcal{R}$ and $\mathcal{L}$ reflect as 
mirrors to a future point $\mathcal{F}$, closing a 
thermodynamic cycle, offers a natural framework for 
connecting the mirror and absorption cases: the mirror 
branch is adiabatic, sources no AS pp-wave, and allows 
interference at $\mathcal{F}$; the absorption branch is 
isothermal, sources the pp-wave, and produces a persistent 
classical record. Gravitational imprinting is precisely 
what breaks the cycle. Whether the Carnot efficiency of 
this cycle connects to the central charge of the 
$\mathrm{BMS}_3$ algebra is an open question. The 
structural identification between the bilocal construction 
and the Penrose--Dirac zig-zag mechanism suggests a 
connection to the origin of mass in the standard model, 
with the Higgs condensate playing the role of $\mathcal{P}$ 
for all massive particles simultaneously. Whether varying 
the microstate boundary action $S_{GHY} = \sum_i 
\hbar\omega_i$ over the ensemble of measurement events 
reproduces the Einstein equations as a stationarity 
condition, deriving GR from the microstate action 
directly, is the primary open problem for future work.
